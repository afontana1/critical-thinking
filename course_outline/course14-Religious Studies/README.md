# Course 13: Religious Studies & Critical Thinking

## Course overview

### Big question

How should we think well about religion—its claims, texts, experiences, institutions, histories, and social functions—without collapsing into either uncritical reverence, shallow dismissal, or cynical relativism?

### By the end, students should be able to

* Distinguish religion as lived practice, religion as a set of truth-claims, religion as social institution, and religion as an object of academic study.
* Explain the main academic approaches to religion and when each is appropriate: philosophical, historical, textual, anthropological, sociological, psychological, and cognitive-scientific.
* Analyze religious beliefs using tools from epistemology: evidence, testimony, faith, belief revision, disagreement, and defeaters.
* Reconstruct and evaluate major arguments for and against the existence of God.
* Distinguish interpretation, doctrine, myth, allegory, narrative, and literal-historical claim.
* Apply historical reasoning to religious origins and development: dating, source criticism, reception, transmission, chronology, authorship, redaction, and canon formation.
* Analyze the difference between describing what a religion teaches, explaining why people believe it, and assessing whether it is true.
* Compare major traditions and religious forms without flattening them into “all basically the same” or “totally incomparable.”
* Evaluate miracle, revelation, prophecy, conversion, and religious experience claims using standards from testimony, probability, and alternative explanation.
* Analyze religious institutions, identity, controversy, secularization, and critique without confusing social influence with truth or falsity.
* Develop a disciplined stance that is neither credulous nor glibly debunking: methodologically pluralist, evidentially calibrated, historically sensitive, and argumentatively fair-minded.

## Unit 1 – What Is Religion? Categories, Boundary Cases, and the Aims of Religious Studies

### Guiding questions

* What are we even studying when we study “religion”?
* Is religion best understood as belief, ritual, identity, institution, experience, worldview, or something else?
* What is the difference between studying religion academically and defending or attacking religion polemically?

### Sub-units

1. Defining religion

   * Belief-centered vs practice-centered vs institution-centered vs family-resemblance approaches.
2. Boundary cases

   * Civil religion, spirituality, secular substitutes, “atheistic religions,” quasi-religious movements.
3. Insider and outsider perspectives

   * Description, interpretation, critique, and advocacy as distinct stances.
4. Why categories matter

   * How definition choices pre-structure later debates.

### Core resources

* Kevin Schilbrack, “The Concept of Religion,” *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*.
* William J. Wainwright, ed., *The Oxford Handbook of Philosophy of Religion*.
* Timothy Fitzgerald, *The Ideology of Religious Studies*.
* Russell T. McCutcheon, *Manufacturing Religion*.

## Unit 2 – How Religion Is Studied: Methods, Explanations, and Levels of Analysis

### Guiding questions

* What counts as an explanation of religion?
* How do philosophy, history, anthropology, sociology, psychology, and cognitive science ask different questions?
* When do methods complement one another, and when do they talk past one another?

### Sub-units

1. Philosophical vs empirical questions

   * Truth, coherence, justification, and metaphysics vs causal and social explanation.
2. Historical and philological methods

   * Sources, languages, redaction, chronology, transmission.
3. Social-scientific approaches

   * Anthropology, sociology, psychology of religion.
4. Cognitive and scientific approaches

   * Cognitive science of religion, ritual, agency detection, meaning-making.

### Core resources

* “Philosophy of Religion,” *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*.
* Helen De Cruz, “Religion and Science,” *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*.
* Clifford Geertz, *The Interpretation of Cultures*.
* Émile Durkheim, *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life*.
* Peter L. Berger, *The Sacred Canopy*.

## Skills labs running through the whole course

* Argument reconstruction: isolate thesis, hidden premises, burden of proof, modal scope, ambiguities, and defeaters.
* Method selection: identify whether a question is primarily philosophical, historical, textual, sociological, psychological, or comparative before trying to answer it.
* Text-status identification: distinguish devotional text, mythic narrative, liturgy, historical source, legal code, allegory, theological reflection, and polemic.
* Miracle/testimony audit: witness quality, independence, prior probability, corroboration, hostile sources, and alternative explanations.
* Historical-source triage: authorship, date, provenance, genre, redaction, manuscript distance, and reception history.
* Comparison discipline: specify which dimension is being compared—god-concept, ritual structure, salvation model, institutional form, cosmology, ethics, or narrative role.
* Belief-update journal: track how one’s assessment changes when new evidence, arguments, or methodological distinctions are introduced.
* Debate without caricature: write the strongest believer, skeptic, and historian versions of a position before assessing it.

## Possible capstone activities

1. **Argument map dossier: one major argument for or against God**
   Reconstruct a major argument in its strongest form, present the strongest objections, then evaluate where the real pressure points are.

2. **Historical case file: a disputed religious origin claim**
   Build a source dossier on a concrete claim involving early Christianity, biblical chronology, miracle tradition, canon formation, or doctrinal development.

3. **Method-comparison paper**
   Take one phenomenon—conversion, ritual, prayer, resurrection belief, scriptural authority, mysticism—and analyze how philosophy, history, anthropology, psychology, and cognitive science each handle it.

4. **Comparative concept analysis**
   Compare one concept across traditions—God, salvation, sacrifice, revelation, liberation, afterlife, sacred law—without flattening differences.

5. **Belief, evidence, and commitment essay**
   Ask whether religious commitment can be rational under conditions of uncertainty, disagreement, and evidential asymmetry.

6. **Public-facing critical thinking guide**
   Write a guide for non-specialists: “How to evaluate religious claims without being gullible, dismissive, or confused.”
