# Critical Questions for Model Thinking

## Course Focus

These questions are designed for **Model Thinking – From System Stories to Explicit Models**. They are meant to help students interrogate models as simplified, purpose-built representations: useful tools for explanation, prediction, exploration, design, communication, and decision support, but never substitutes for reality itself.

The central aim is to help students ask not only **“What does the model say?”** but also:

- What question was this model built to answer?
- What has been simplified, omitted, idealized, or assumed?
- What kind of model is being used, and why this kind rather than another?
- How does the model connect to data, uncertainty, sensitivity, validation, ethics, and power?
- What would responsible use of this model require?

---

## 1. Foundational Questions: What Is a Model?

### Models as representations

1. What aspect of the world is this model trying to represent?
2. What question is the model built to answer?
3. What does the model deliberately simplify?
4. What does the model leave out entirely?
5. What does the model treat as stable, fixed, or irrelevant?
6. What does the model treat as variable, dynamic, or uncertain?
7. What is the target system of the model?
8. What scale does the model operate at: individual, group, institution, ecosystem, economy, or society?
9. What time horizon does the model assume?
10. What would count as a successful use of this model?
11. What would count as a misuse of this model?
12. Is the model being used to explain, predict, explore, design, decide, or communicate?
13. Are those purposes being kept distinct, or are they being blurred?
14. Does the model clarify something that would otherwise remain vague?
15. Does the model hide something that should remain visible?

### Mental models and formal models

16. What implicit mental model is already shaping how people understand this situation?
17. What would be gained by making that mental model explicit?
18. What changes when a mental model becomes a diagram, equation, simulation, table, or decision model?
19. Who can inspect, challenge, or revise the model?
20. Is the model public enough to be criticized?
21. What assumptions are easier to see once the model is formalized?
22. What assumptions may become harder to see because the model looks technical or authoritative?
23. Does formalizing the model improve accountability?
24. Does formalizing the model create a false sense of precision?
25. What parts of the model depend on judgment rather than measurement?

### Why model?

26. What can this model help us see that ordinary description cannot?
27. What can this model help us test that unaided intuition cannot?
28. What can this model help us compare that narrative alone cannot?
29. What can this model help us communicate across disciplines or stakeholders?
30. What kinds of questions are inappropriate for this model?
31. Is the model being asked to do more than it was built to do?
32. Is the model a tool for inquiry or a tool for persuasion?
33. Is the model helping people think, or replacing their thinking?
34. Does the model invite revision, or does it close down discussion?
35. What would we lose if we had no model at all?

## 2. Model Land, Idealization, and Limits

### Model Land versus the real world

36. What is true inside the model’s simplified world that may not be true in the real world?
37. What assumptions define the model’s “Model Land”?
38. Which real-world complications have been excluded for clarity?
39. Which excluded complications might actually dominate the outcome?
40. Does the model assume an open system behaves like a closed system?
41. Does the model ignore feedback, adaptation, or changing behavior?
42. Does the model assume stable relationships where relationships may change over time?
43. Does the model treat people as simpler than they are?
44. Does the model treat institutions as more coherent than they are?
45. What would it mean to “escape” from the model and return to the messy world?

### Idealization and abstraction

46. Which idealizations are harmless for this purpose?
47. Which idealizations are risky or potentially misleading?
48. What does the model abstract away from in order to become usable?
49. Is the abstraction appropriate to the question?
50. Does the model simplify mechanisms, agents, environments, or values?
51. Does the model collapse important heterogeneity into averages?
52. Does the model ignore rare but important cases?
53. Does the model assume linear relationships where thresholds or nonlinearities matter?
54. Does the model ignore delays or path dependence?
55. How would the conclusions change if the abstraction were relaxed?

### Explanation, prediction, and control

56. Is the model explanatory, predictive, exploratory, or prescriptive?
57. Is a model that explains well being wrongly treated as if it predicts well?
58. Is a model that predicts well being wrongly treated as if it explains causally?
59. Is an exploratory scenario being mistaken for a forecast?
60. Is a decision-support model being treated as a decision-maker?
61. Does the model show what might happen, what will happen, or what should happen?
62. Are users clear about that distinction?
63. What would responsible communication of the model’s purpose require?
64. What kind of confidence is warranted by the model’s output?
65. Where does the model’s authority end?

### Scope and applicability

66. What is the domain of applicability of this model?
67. Under what conditions does the model work reasonably well?
68. Under what conditions would it likely fail?
69. What population, context, or historical period does the model assume?
70. Is the model being transported to a new setting without justification?
71. Does the model require background conditions that may not hold elsewhere?
72. How sensitive is the model to context?
73. Is the model local, general, or aspirationally universal?
74. What warnings should accompany the model when it travels?
75. Who is responsible for explaining the model’s limits?

## 3. Types of Models and Multi-Model Thinking

### Choosing a model family

76. What kind of model is this: statistical, mechanistic, network, optimization, agent-based, game-theoretic, system dynamics, or something else?
77. Why was this model family chosen?
78. What kind of question is this model family especially good at answering?
79. What kind of question is this model family bad at answering?
80. Does the model family fit the structure of the problem?
81. Is the problem mainly about prediction, mechanism, interaction, strategy, allocation, or feedback?
82. Would a different model family reveal something important?
83. What is the cost of using this model rather than another?
84. What assumptions come bundled with this model type?
85. Are those assumptions defensible in this case?

### Statistical models

86. Is the model describing patterns in data or representing a system that produces those patterns?
87. What variables are included, and why?
88. What variables are excluded, and why?
89. Does the model confuse correlation with causation?
90. Does the model summarize the past in a way that may fail in the future?
91. Does the model depend on stable relationships across contexts?
92. Does the model overfit noise?
93. Does the model underfit important structure?
94. Are predictions being interpreted as explanations?
95. Are uncertainty intervals or error estimates communicated honestly?

### Mechanistic and system dynamics models

96. What mechanism does the model represent?
97. What stocks, flows, feedback loops, and delays are included?
98. What mechanisms are left implicit or omitted?
99. Does the model represent causal production or merely patterned association?
100. Does the model capture the behavior-over-time pattern that needs explanation?
101. Are feedback loops modeled explicitly enough?
102. Are delays represented realistically?
103. Does the model allow for saturation, limits, or collapse?
104. Does the model clarify the structure behind the observed pattern?
105. What mechanism would falsify or weaken the model’s explanation?

### Network models

106. What are the nodes?
107. What are the links?
108. What does a link mean: contact, influence, dependency, trust, exchange, contagion, similarity, or something else?
109. Are the links directed or undirected?
110. Are all links treated as equal when some are stronger or weaker?
111. Are hubs, clusters, bridges, or bottlenecks important?
112. Does the network change over time?
113. Does the model capture cascades or contagion?
114. What gets hidden by representing the system as a network?
115. How would the analysis change if the network boundary were redrawn?

### Agent-based models

116. Who or what are the agents?
117. What states can agents occupy?
118. What rules govern agent behavior?
119. What rules govern agent interaction?
120. What environment do agents operate in?
121. What forms of heterogeneity matter among agents?
122. Do agents adapt, learn, imitate, or respond strategically?
123. What macro-pattern emerges from micro-rules?
124. Are the rules empirically grounded, theoretical, or speculative?
125. Is the emergent pattern robust across many runs and parameter settings?

### Optimization and decision models

126. What decision variables are under control?
127. What objective is being maximized or minimized?
128. Who chose the objective?
129. What constraints are included?
130. What constraints are treated as fixed but could actually be changed?
131. What values are embedded in the objective function?
132. What tradeoffs are hidden by a single objective?
133. Are equity, fairness, resilience, or long-term effects excluded from the optimization?
134. What happens if the metric becomes the target?
135. Does the optimized solution remain sensible outside the model?

### Game-theoretic and strategic models

136. Who are the players?
137. What are their goals, incentives, and available strategies?
138. What information does each player have?
139. Are players assumed to be rational in a way that fits the case?
140. Does the model account for trust, norms, bounded rationality, or emotion?
141. Are repeated interactions important?
142. Does the model assume fixed preferences when preferences may change?
143. What equilibrium concept is being used, explicitly or implicitly?
144. Does the model explain strategic interaction or oversimplify it?
145. What real-world behavior would challenge the strategic assumptions?

### Multi-model thinking

146. What would a second model reveal that the first model misses?
147. What would a third model challenge in the first two?
148. Do different models converge on the same conclusion?
149. If models disagree, what exactly do they disagree about?
150. Is the disagreement caused by data, assumptions, structure, scale, values, or purpose?
151. Does model diversity reduce overconfidence?
152. Does an ensemble clarify uncertainty or obscure accountability?
153. When is model pluralism intellectually responsible?
154. When is adding more models merely confusing?
155. What would it mean to triangulate across models?

## 4. From System Stories to Explicit Models

### From problem to model purpose

156. What system story is the model trying to make explicit?

160. Which mechanisms can safely remain outside the model?

162. What inputs or assumptions drive those outputs?
163. What decisions or interventions will the model help compare?
164. What would the model need to show to be useful?

### Variables and aggregation

166. What are the state variables?
167. What are the parameters?
168. What are the control variables?
169. What are the exogenous drivers?
170. Which variables are measured directly?
171. Which variables are proxies?
172. Which variables are conceptual or latent?
173. What aggregation choices are being made?
174. What is gained by aggregation?
175. What is lost by aggregation?

### Translating diagrams into rules

176. How does a causal loop diagram become a model structure?
177. How does a stock–flow diagram become a set of update rules?
178. How does a network diagram become a model of diffusion, influence, or dependency?
179. How does an interaction story become agent rules?
180. Where does judgment enter the translation from story to model?
181. Does the formal version preserve the original system insight?
182. Does formalization distort the original system story?
183. Are feedback loops made explicit or accidentally removed?

### Toy models

186. What is the toy model designed to teach?
187. What mechanism does the toy model isolate?
188. What is deliberately unrealistic about the toy model?
189. Does the toy model clarify or oversimplify?
190. What should students not infer from the toy model?

192. Does the toy model generate surprising behavior from simple assumptions?
193. What real-world cases resemble the toy model only partially?

## 5. Data, Calibration, Validation, and Model Evidence

### Models and data

196. What data does the model use?
197. What data does the model ignore?
198. What data would be needed to test the model better?
199. Are the data measuring the variables the model actually needs?
200. Are proxies being treated as if they were the real target?
201. What measurement errors might affect model inputs?
202. What sampling biases might affect model calibration?
203. What missing data might distort the model?
204. Does the model assume the data-generating process is stable?
205. Does the model make the data more interpretable or merely more impressive?

### Calibration and fitting

206. Which parameters are chosen from theory?
207. Which parameters are estimated from data?
208. Which parameters are adjusted mainly to improve fit?
209. How many “knobs” does the model have?
210. Is the model fitting signal or noise?
211. What would overfitting look like in this case?
212. What would underfitting look like?
213. Are calibration data independent from validation data?
214. Would the model perform well on new cases?
215. Does good fit imply good explanation?

### Validation and confirmation

216. What does it mean to validate this model?
217. What kind of validation is possible?
218. What kind of validation is impossible?
219. Has the model been compared against data not used to build it?
220. Has the model been tested in different contexts?
221. Does the model reproduce known patterns for the right reasons?
222. Could the model get the right answer for the wrong reason?
223. What failures would be informative?
224. What would count as serious evidence against the model?
225. Are users overstating what validation can prove?

### Models as arguments

226. What are the model’s premises?
227. What is the model’s inferential structure?
228. What conclusions are drawn from the model outputs?
229. Which conclusions follow strongly from the model?
230. Which conclusions require additional assumptions?
231. Are outputs being interpreted beyond what the model supports?
232. Does the model function like an argument with hidden premises?
233. Are the hidden premises visible to non-experts?
234. What objections would weaken the model-based argument?
235. How would you reconstruct the model’s reasoning in plain language?

## 6. Uncertainty, Sensitivity, and Robustness

### Types of uncertainty

236. What parameter uncertainty affects the model?
237. What structural uncertainty affects the model?
238. What scenario uncertainty affects the model?
239. What measurement uncertainty affects the model?
240. What uncertainty comes from human behavior or institutional change?
241. Which uncertainties can be quantified?
242. Which uncertainties can only be described qualitatively?
243. Are unknown unknowns acknowledged?
244. Is uncertainty being narrowed for convenience rather than understanding?
245. How should uncertainty be communicated to decision-makers?

### Sensitivity analysis

246. Which assumptions most strongly affect the results?
247. Which parameters have little effect on the conclusions?
248. What happens if a key assumption is doubled, halved, or reversed?
249. Are results fragile or stable under plausible changes?
250. Does sensitivity analysis reveal hidden leverage points?
251. Does sensitivity analysis reveal false precision?
252. Are only convenient assumptions being varied?
253. Are structural assumptions tested, or only numerical parameters?
254. What would a global sensitivity analysis reveal that one-at-a-time variation might miss?
255. How should sensitivity results change model interpretation?

### Robustness

256. What conclusions survive across plausible assumptions?
257. What conclusions survive across multiple model types?
258. What conclusions survive across different scenarios?
259. What decisions perform acceptably across many futures?
260. What decisions depend on one fragile forecast?
261. Does the model encourage optimization at the expense of robustness?
262. Does the model identify strategies that are good enough across uncertainty?
263. What tradeoff exists between efficiency and resilience?
264. What would stress testing reveal?
265. What should users do if the model is too uncertain for precise prediction but still useful for exploration?

7. What data support the model?
8. How was it calibrated?
9. How was it checked or validated?
10. What uncertainties remain?
