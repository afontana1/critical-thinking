# Critical Questions for Course 2: Argumentation Fundamentals

## Purpose of This Question Bank

This document collects critical questions aligned with the course **Argumentation Fundamentals**. The questions are designed to help students analyze, evaluate, reconstruct, and construct arguments responsibly across everyday, scientific, legal, policy, interpretive, and public contexts.

The goal is not merely to help students “win arguments” or memorize fallacy names. The goal is to help them understand how arguments work: how reasons support conclusions, how schemes function, how evidence interacts with hypotheses and stories, how defeasible reasoning operates, how burdens of proof shift, how appeals to authority can be reasonable or abusive, and how argument analysis connects to intellectual virtue, language, framing, and social trust.

These questions may be used for class discussion, reading responses, argument maps, writing assignments, debate preparation, peer review, exams, or capstone projects.

# I. Foundational Questions About Argument

## A. What Counts as an Argument?

1. What is the difference between an argument, an assertion, an explanation, a narrative, and a disagreement?
2. When someone gives a reason, how can we tell whether that reason is meant to justify a conclusion or merely explain why something happened?
3. Can a passage contain argumentative force even if it does not use words like “therefore,” “because,” or “so”?
4. What hidden conclusion might a speaker be trying to lead an audience toward?
5. What is the difference between persuading someone and giving them good reasons?
6. Can an argument be rhetorically effective but logically weak?
7. Can an argument be logically strong but rhetorically ineffective?
8. What makes an argument intellectually responsible rather than merely persuasive?
9. How does the context of a conversation determine what counts as a relevant reason?
10. What background assumptions must be shared before this argument can even get started?
11. Is the argument trying to establish what is true, what is likely, what should be done, what something means, or whom we should trust?
12. What kind of argumentative success is being sought: proof, justification, explanation, persuasion, refutation, clarification, or decision?
13. Does the argument aim to settle a question, shift a burden, create doubt, motivate action, or reframe the issue?
14. Is the argument operating in a cooperative search for truth, an adversarial dispute, a legal setting, a political campaign, or an informal conversation?
15. How would the standards for evaluating the argument change across those contexts?

## B. Reasons, Conclusions, and Support

1. What exactly is the main conclusion?
2. Which claims function as reasons for that conclusion?
3. Which claims are background assumptions rather than stated premises?
4. Are there intermediate conclusions that function both as conclusions and as premises?
5. Are the premises independent, linked, convergent, or serial?
6. Does each premise actually support the conclusion, or merely seem associated with it?
7. Which premise does the most argumentative work?
8. Which premise is most vulnerable to objection?
9. Could the conclusion still stand if one premise were removed?
10. Does the argument rely on an unstated warrant connecting the evidence to the claim?
11. Is the support deductive, inductive, abductive, analogical, presumptive, practical, interpretive, or testimonial?
12. Is the conclusion presented with an appropriate degree of confidence?
13. Are qualifiers such as “probably,” “usually,” “in most cases,” or “unless” being used responsibly?
14. Does the arguer acknowledge possible exceptions, rebuttals, or defeaters?
15. What would make the argument stronger without changing its conclusion?

## C. Argument Reconstruction

1. What is the most charitable version of this argument?
2. What is the least charitable but still plausible version?
3. Which reconstruction best fits the text and context?
4. Are we adding missing premises that the arguer would actually accept?
5. Are we imposing a structure on the argument that distorts it?
6. Does the reconstruction preserve the arguer’s intended meaning?
7. Does it make the inferential relationships explicit?
8. Does it distinguish between evidence, warrant, backing, qualifier, and rebuttal?
9. Does it separate the argument itself from surrounding rhetoric, narrative, or emotional appeal?
10. Does the reconstruction reveal hidden assumptions that were previously invisible?
11. Are we reconstructing the argument as deductive when it was meant to be defeasible?
12. Are we treating a presumptive argument as if it had to prove its conclusion absolutely?
13. Does the argument require a map rather than a simple premise-conclusion list?
14. What alternative maps of the same argument are possible?
15. Which reconstruction makes the argument easiest to evaluate fairly?

# II. Toulmin Model and Argument Mapping

## A. Toulmin-Style Questions

1. What is the claim being advanced?
2. What data or grounds are offered in support of the claim?
3. What warrant connects the grounds to the claim?
4. Is the warrant explicit or implicit?
5. What backing supports the warrant?
6. Is the warrant generally acceptable in this context?
7. Does the warrant rely on a definition, analogy, value judgment, social norm, expert consensus, or causal assumption?
8. What qualifier indicates the strength of the claim?
9. Is the claim overqualified, underqualified, or appropriately qualified?
10. What rebuttals or exceptions does the arguer acknowledge?
11. What rebuttals does the arguer ignore?
12. Does the argument’s backing support the warrant or merely restate the claim?
13. Does the argument confuse data with warrant?
14. Does the argument use strong evidence with a weak warrant?
15. Does the argument use a plausible warrant but insufficient grounds?
16. What burden of proof is implied by the claim’s strength?
17. Does the arguer meet that burden?
18. Would the argument change if the qualifier were made more modest?
19. What would count as a successful rebuttal to this argument?
20. Does the Toulmin layout reveal a hidden weakness that was not obvious in prose?

## B. Argument Mapping Questions

1. What are the main branches of support in the argument?
2. Which reasons are co-premises that must work together?
3. Which reasons independently support the same conclusion?
4. Where do objections enter the map?
5. Are there rebuttals to objections?
6. Are there objections to rebuttals?
7. Does the map distinguish between attacking a premise and attacking an inference?
8. Does the map show where the argument depends on unstated assumptions?
9. Are any premises doing duplicate work?
10. Are any claims floating without support?
11. Does the visual structure make the argument look stronger than it is?
12. Does the map hide ambiguity by forcing a linear structure onto a complex dispute?
13. What competing map could represent the same text differently?
14. Which part of the map would be most important to investigate further?
15. How does mapping change your understanding of the argument?
16. Does the map help identify where disagreement actually lies?
17. Does the map reveal that two sides disagree about facts, values, definitions, warrants, or burdens?
18. Does the map make room for defeaters and exceptions?
19. Can the argument be repaired by adding missing support?
20. What would a fair map of the opposing argument look like?

## C. Argument Mining Questions

1. Which sentences in the text are argumentative rather than descriptive?
2. Which claims are evidence, which are conclusions, and which are rhetorical framing?
3. What argument indicators are present, if any?
4. What argumentative moves are implied rather than explicitly stated?
5. Are multiple arguments being blended together?
6. Are arguments embedded inside stories, examples, or emotional appeals?
7. What would be lost if the argument were extracted from its rhetorical context?
8. What would be gained by extracting it?
9. Does the text contain implicit objections or anticipated rebuttals?
10. Does the arguer shift from one conclusion to another without signaling the shift?
11. Is the text arguing for a belief, an action, a classification, or an interpretation?
12. Does the argument mining process risk oversimplifying the speaker’s position?
13. How should ambiguous claims be represented in a map?
14. What human judgment is required before computational tools can analyze the argument well?
15. What features of natural language make argument mining difficult?

# III. Argumentation Schemes and Critical Questions

## A. General Scheme Questions

1. What argumentation scheme best fits this argument?
2. Is more than one scheme operating at once?
3. What are the standard critical questions for this scheme?
4. Which critical question is most important in this case?
5. Has the arguer answered the relevant critical questions?
6. Has the arguer ignored predictable objections?
7. Is the scheme being used legitimately or abused?
8. Is the argument weak because the scheme is weak, or because this instance misuses the scheme?
9. Is the conclusion stronger than the scheme can support?
10. Does the arguer treat a presumptive scheme as if it produced certainty?
11. Does the argument depend on context-sensitive norms that need to be stated?
12. What would a competing argument using the same scheme look like?
13. What would a competing argument using a different scheme look like?
14. Does the scheme help clarify the argument, or does it artificially categorize it?
15. How does identifying the scheme help us repair the argument?

## B. Scheme Misuse Questions

1. What legitimate form of reasoning is being distorted?
2. Is a weak analogy being presented as a decisive comparison?
3. Is an appeal to authority being used outside the authority’s domain?
4. Is a practical argument ignoring side effects or alternative means?
5. Is an argument from consequences being confused with evidence for truth?
6. Is an argument from ignorance being used without a competent search?
7. Is a causal argument ignoring alternative causes?
8. Is a sign argument treating a possible indicator as conclusive proof?
9. Is a popular opinion being treated as expert consensus?
10. Is an argument from precedent ignoring relevant disanalogies?
11. Is the arguer shifting schemes midstream?
12. Is the arguer exploiting the audience’s familiarity with one scheme to smuggle in another?
13. Does the argument look formally tight while relying on vague or contestable premises?
14. What critical question would expose the misuse most directly?
15. How could the argument be reformulated into a legitimate version of the scheme?

# IV. Defeasible and Non-Monotonic Reasoning

## A. Defeasibility Questions

1. Is this argument deductive, or is it defeasible?
2. What new information could defeat the conclusion?
3. What exceptions are already known?
4. Are exceptions rare, common, or central to the issue?
5. Does the arguer acknowledge that the inference is defeasible?
6. Does the audience mistake defeasible support for proof?
7. Does the argument depend on a default assumption?
8. Is the default assumption reasonable in this context?
9. Has the default been defeated by specific evidence?
10. What would restore the default after a defeater is introduced?
11. Are we dealing with a rebutting defeater or an undercutting defeater?
12. Does new information attack the conclusion or the connection between premise and conclusion?
13. How does the argument change when background conditions change?
14. Does the argument remain strong under normal circumstances but fail in this special case?
15. What makes a defeasible inference rational even though it is not certain?

## B. Monotonic vs. Non-Monotonic Questions

1. Would adding new premises ever force us to withdraw this conclusion?
2. If yes, why is classical deductive validity not the right model for this reasoning?
3. What default rule is being applied?
4. What are the known exceptions to that default rule?
5. Does the argument depend on incomplete information?
6. Is the arguer reasoning responsibly from incomplete information?
7. Does the conclusion need to be withdrawn, weakened, or qualified after new information appears?
8. Can the argument be represented as a default rule plus exceptions?
9. Are there competing defaults in tension with one another?
10. Which default should have priority in this context?
11. How do presumptions help us reason without proving everything from scratch?
12. How do defeaters prevent presumptions from becoming dogma?
13. How does non-monotonic reasoning explain ordinary legal, medical, and scientific reasoning?
14. What goes wrong when people demand deductive certainty in contexts that require defeasible judgment?
15. What goes wrong when people use defeasibility as an excuse for irresponsibility?

# V. Analogical Arguments and Precedent

## A. Basic Analogy Questions

1. What two cases are being compared?
2. What conclusion is the analogy supposed to support?
3. What properties do the cases allegedly share?
4. Which shared properties are relevant to the conclusion?
5. Which shared properties are superficial?
6. What important differences between the cases are being ignored?
7. Are the differences stronger than the similarities?
8. Is the analogy being used to explain or to argue?
9. Does the analogy clarify the issue or distort it?
10. Does the analogy import emotional associations from one case into another?
11. Does the analogy depend on a metaphor inherited from Course 1?
12. Is the analogy doing evidential work or merely framing work?
13. What alternative analogy would lead to a different conclusion?
14. Is the analogy chosen because it is accurate or because it is rhetorically powerful?
15. What would make this analogy more precise?

## B. Story Similarity Questions

1. Are the compared cases similar as stories rather than merely as feature lists?
2. Do the stories share a causal structure?
3. Do they share a moral structure?
4. Do they assign similar roles such as victim, aggressor, rescuer, authority, or bystander?
5. Are those roles justified by the facts?
6. Does the analogy depend on who is cast as the hero or villain?
7. Does the narrative structure make one conclusion feel natural before evidence is evaluated?
8. Are we comparing events at the right level of abstraction?
9. Is the similarity based on goals, motives, consequences, institutional context, or emotional tone?
10. Does the analogy suppress a different story that could be told about the same case?
11. Does the analogy rely on a familiar cultural script?
12. What story elements must be true for the analogy to work?
13. Are those story elements established or assumed?
14. Does the analogy collapse complex cases into simplified moral templates?
15. How would the argument change if the same facts were narrated differently?

## C. Precedent and Case-Based Reasoning Questions

1. What previous case is being used as a precedent?
2. What rule, principle, or lesson is being drawn from the precedent?
3. Is the precedent binding, persuasive, illustrative, or merely rhetorical?
4. Are the current case and precedent relevantly similar?
5. What features of the precedent are legally, morally, or practically significant?
6. What disanalogies might justify treating the current case differently?
7. Is the precedent being extended, limited, distinguished, or overturned?
8. Is the argument relying on the authority of the precedent or the reasoning behind it?
9. Does the precedent itself rest on a questionable classification or definition?
10. Are there competing precedents that point in another direction?
11. Which precedent is more analogous, and why?
12. Does the arguer cherry-pick precedents while ignoring contrary cases?
13. Does the argument confuse “this happened before” with “this should happen again”?
14. How does precedent stabilize reasoning while also risking conservatism or injustice?
15. When should like cases be treated alike, and when should a category be revised?

## D. Abusive Analogy Questions

1. Is the analogy designed to inflame rather than illuminate?
2. Does it compare ordinary opponents to extreme villains without sufficient justification?
3. Does it smuggle in moral condemnation through comparison rather than argument?
4. Does it rely on a historically loaded analogy that overwhelms careful analysis?
5. Does it silence nuance by making dissent seem morally impossible?
6. Does the analogy encourage guilt by association?
7. What precise similarity is being claimed?
8. Is that similarity actually relevant to the conclusion?
9. Does the analogy erase important historical, institutional, or causal differences?
10. What would a responsible version of the analogy look like?

# VI. Abduction and Explanatory Inference

## A. Abduction Basics

1. What phenomenon is being explained?
2. What hypothesis is proposed to explain it?
3. How does abduction differ from deduction in this case?
4. How does it differ from simple induction?
5. Is the argument moving from data to a possible explanation or to the best explanation?
6. What makes the hypothesis explanatory rather than merely compatible with the data?
7. Does the hypothesis explain all the relevant facts or only selected ones?
8. Are there facts the hypothesis fails to explain?
9. Does the argument confuse “could explain” with “does explain”?
10. Does the explanation require too many auxiliary assumptions?
11. Is the proposed explanation independently plausible?
12. Does the explanation fit with background knowledge?
13. Does the explanation generate testable expectations?
14. What observations would be surprising if the hypothesis were true?
15. What observations would be surprising if the hypothesis were false?

## B. Inference to the Best Explanation Questions

1. What are the competing explanations?
2. Has the arguer considered enough alternatives?
3. Which explanation has the greatest explanatory power?
4. Which explanation is simplest without being simplistic?
5. Which explanation best unifies the evidence?
6. Which explanation fits best with well-established background knowledge?
7. Which explanation is most ad hoc?
8. Which explanation requires the fewest independent coincidences?
9. Which explanation accounts for both expected and unexpected evidence?
10. Which explanation survives the strongest objections?
11. Are we privileging an explanation because it is emotionally satisfying?
12. Are we mistaking narrative coherence for truth?
13. Does the “best” explanation depend on a restricted or biased hypothesis space?
14. Are there unconceived alternatives we have not imagined?
15. How should confidence change if a new plausible explanation appears?

## C. Plausibility and Hypothesis Space Questions

1. What makes a hypothesis seem plausible to this audience?
2. Is plausibility being shaped by evidence, familiarity, ideology, or framing?
3. What explanations are excluded before inquiry even begins?
4. Who gets to define the set of serious hypotheses?
5. Are some explanations dismissed because they are genuinely weak or because they violate group assumptions?
6. Are some explanations accepted because they fit an existing narrative?
7. Is the hypothesis space artificially narrow?
8. Is it so broad that no discrimination is possible?
9. What background beliefs control which explanations seem live?
10. How do institutions, experts, media, or echo chambers influence the hypothesis space?
11. Are we updating beliefs in response to evidence or merely adding evidence to a preferred story?
12. Does the evidence favor one hypothesis over another, or merely make one hypothesis vivid?
13. Are prior probabilities being ignored?
14. Is the argument vulnerable to cherry-picked evidence?
15. What additional evidence would most efficiently discriminate between hypotheses?

## D. Abduction in Everyday Life, Science, and Law

1. How does a detective, scientist, physician, or historian use abductive reasoning differently?
2. What standards of explanation are appropriate in this context?
3. Is the explanation meant to guide further inquiry or justify final belief?
4. Are we still in the hypothesis-generation stage or the hypothesis-testing stage?
5. Does the explanation fit the evidence better than rival explanations?
6. Is the explanation being treated as proven too early?
7. Is uncertainty being communicated clearly?
8. What role do expert communities play in filtering explanations?
9. What dangers arise when laypeople perform abduction without domain knowledge?
10. How can abductive reasoning become conspiratorial when it overvalues pattern detection?

# VII. Practical Reasoning and Decision

## A. Means-End Reasoning Questions

1. What goal is the action supposed to achieve?
2. Is the goal explicit or implicit?
3. Is the goal legitimate, shared, or contested?
4. Does the proposed action actually promote the goal?
5. What evidence supports the means-end connection?
6. Are there alternative actions that would promote the goal better?
7. Are there hidden costs, risks, or side effects?
8. Does the action undermine another important goal?
9. Is the argument ignoring opportunity costs?
10. Does the action solve the stated problem or merely appear decisive?
11. Is the goal being used to justify means that conflict with deeper values?
12. Is the proposed action proportionate to the problem?
13. What would count as failure after the action is taken?
14. What monitoring or feedback would be needed to revise the decision?
15. Does the practical argument include an exit condition or stopping rule?

## B. Value-Based Practical Reasoning Questions

1. What values are being invoked?
2. Are those values named openly or smuggled in implicitly?
3. Are the values shared by all parties?
4. What happens when the values conflict?
5. Which value is being prioritized, and why?
6. Does the argument treat a contested value as obvious?
7. Does the argument reduce a plural value conflict to a single metric?
8. Are rights, welfare, fairness, liberty, security, equality, dignity, or efficiency in tension?
9. Does the argument distinguish instrumental goods from intrinsic goods?
10. Does it confuse what is useful with what is right?
11. Does the proposed action express the values it claims to serve?
12. Are some affected parties excluded from the value calculation?
13. Does the argument depend on an unexamined conception of human flourishing?
14. Would the argument look different from the standpoint of a less powerful group?
15. How should disagreement about values be handled responsibly?

## C. Decision Theory Questions

1. What are the available options?
2. Are important options missing from the menu?
3. What outcomes might result from each option?
4. How likely are those outcomes?
5. How valuable or harmful would those outcomes be?
6. Are probabilities being estimated responsibly?
7. Are utilities being treated as measurable when they may be contested?
8. Are low-probability high-impact outcomes being ignored?
9. Is expected utility the right framework for this decision?
10. Are risk, uncertainty, and ignorance being distinguished?
11. Does the decision involve moral constraints that should not be reduced to utility?
12. Are preferences stable, informed, and ethically defensible?
13. How do cognitive limitations affect the decision process?
14. What role should precaution play when evidence is uncertain?
15. What would a transparent decision process look like?

## D. Policy Deliberation Questions

1. What problem is the policy supposed to solve?
2. How is the problem defined?
3. Who benefits from that problem definition?
4. What evidence shows that the policy would work?
5. What evidence shows that it might fail?
6. What unintended consequences are foreseeable?
7. Who bears the risks and who receives the benefits?
8. What tradeoffs are being minimized or hidden?
9. Does the argument appeal to cost-benefit analysis, rights, justice, security, public health, or tradition?
10. Are those frameworks compatible in this case?
11. What alternative policies have been considered?
12. Is the burden of proof appropriate given the policy’s risks?
13. Does the argument exploit urgency to bypass scrutiny?
14. Does it exploit uncertainty to prevent action?
15. What would make the policy argument more accountable and revisable?

# VIII. Presumption, Presupposition, Assumption, and Burden of Proof

## A. Presumption Questions

1. What is being presumed at the start of the argument?
2. Why is that presumption reasonable in this context?
3. Is the presumption legal, conversational, practical, moral, or evidential?
4. Who benefits from this presumption?
5. Who is disadvantaged by it?
6. What would defeat the presumption?
7. Has defeating evidence already been introduced?
8. Is the presumption being treated as conclusive rather than defeasible?
9. Does the argument rely on a default that may not hold in this context?
10. Is the presumption necessary because proving everything from scratch is impossible?
11. Is the presumption being used to avoid having to provide evidence?
12. Is the presumption fair to all parties?
13. Should the presumption shift as new evidence enters the dialogue?
14. What happens if we reverse the presumption?
15. Would the argument still seem strong if the burden of proof were assigned differently?

## B. Assumption Questions

1. What assumptions does the argument require?
2. Which assumptions are stated?
3. Which assumptions are hidden?
4. Which assumptions are harmless simplifications?
5. Which assumptions are controversial?
6. Which assumptions are necessary for the conclusion?
7. Which assumptions could be removed without affecting the argument?
8. Are assumptions being made for the sake of argument or presented as fact?
9. Has the arguer earned the right to rely on those assumptions?
10. Are the assumptions consistent with one another?
11. Do the assumptions reflect a particular worldview or ideology?
12. Are the assumptions empirical, conceptual, normative, or interpretive?
13. What evidence would test the assumptions?
14. What happens if a key assumption is false?
15. How could the assumptions be made explicit for fair evaluation?

## C. Presupposition Questions

1. What must already be accepted for the statement to make sense?
2. Does the statement presuppose something controversial?
3. Does the question contain a trap, such as “When did you stop doing X?”
4. Is a contested claim being smuggled in as background?
5. Does the presupposition survive denial?
6. Is the speaker relying on factive language such as “realize,” “admit,” or “regret”?
7. Does the utterance presuppose guilt, responsibility, agreement, or legitimacy?
8. How does the presupposition shape what answers are available?
9. Does challenging the presupposition look evasive even when it is necessary?
10. Who controls the conversational frame through presupposition?
11. Are presuppositions functioning like hidden premises?
12. Are they functioning like social pressures?
13. What would a neutral version of the question look like?
14. How can one challenge a presupposition without derailing dialogue?
15. How do presuppositions connect to framing and doublespeak from Course 1?

## D. Burden of Proof Questions

1. Who currently bears the burden of proof?
2. Why is the burden assigned that way?
3. Is the burden legal, scientific, conversational, or moral?
4. What standard of proof applies?
5. Is the standard too high, too low, or appropriate?
6. Has the arguer shifted the burden unfairly?
7. Is someone demanding that others disprove an unsupported claim?
8. Is skepticism being used responsibly or as a way to avoid commitment?
9. Does the seriousness of the claim affect the burden?
10. Does the risk of action or inaction affect the burden?
11. Is lack of proof being confused with proof of falsehood?
12. Is failure to disprove being confused with proof of truth?
13. How should burden of proof operate under uncertainty?
14. When should presumptions shift during a dialogue?
15. What would a fair distribution of burdens look like?

# IX. Evidence and Story-Based Reasoning

## A. Evidence Function Questions

1. What is being offered as evidence?
2. Evidence for what conclusion?
3. Is the evidence direct or indirect?
4. Is it testimonial, statistical, experimental, observational, documentary, circumstantial, or expert-based?
5. How reliable is the source of the evidence?
6. How relevant is the evidence to the claim?
7. How strong is the connection between evidence and conclusion?
8. Is the evidence being overinterpreted?
9. Is the evidence compatible with multiple hypotheses?
10. Is the evidence being used to support a story, a causal claim, a classification, or a policy recommendation?
11. Does the argument satisfy the total evidence requirement?
12. What relevant evidence is missing?
13. Has contrary evidence been acknowledged?
14. Is the evidence cherry-picked?
15. What additional evidence would most improve evaluation?

## B. Evidence-to-Hypothesis Questions

1. What hypothesis is the evidence supposed to support?
2. Does the evidence make the hypothesis more likely than before?
3. Does it make the hypothesis more likely than rival hypotheses?
4. Is the evidence expected if the hypothesis is true?
5. Is the evidence also expected if a rival hypothesis is true?
6. Does the hypothesis predict the evidence, or was it invented after the fact?
7. Is the evidence independent of the hypothesis?
8. Is the evidence reliable enough to bear the weight placed on it?
9. Is background knowledge being used responsibly?
10. Are base rates being ignored?
11. Are we confusing correlation, sign, cause, and explanation?
12. Does the evidence support the whole hypothesis or only a weaker claim?
13. Does the argument move too quickly from “consistent with” to “confirms”?
14. What would count as disconfirming evidence?
15. How should confidence be calibrated after considering the evidence?

## C. Story-Based Reasoning Questions

1. What story is being constructed from the evidence?
2. Does the story have internal coherence?
3. Does internal coherence make it true?
4. What facts are included in the story?
5. What facts are omitted?
6. Does the story explain the evidence better than rival stories?
7. Are narrative gaps filled by evidence or imagination?
8. Does the story rely on stereotypes or familiar scripts?
9. Does the story assign roles before the evidence justifies them?
10. Does the story make some evidence seem more relevant than other evidence?
11. Is the story emotionally compelling but evidentially weak?
12. Does the narrative structure bias the interpretation of ambiguous facts?
13. Can the same evidence be arranged into a competing story?
14. What argument map supports the story?
15. What objections threaten the story?

## D. Legal and Scientific Evidence Questions

1. How do legal standards of evidence differ from scientific standards?
2. Is the goal to establish liability, causation, guilt, mechanism, prediction, or explanation?
3. What standard of proof applies in this setting?
4. Is testimony being treated appropriately?
5. Are expert reports being weighed against other kinds of evidence?
6. Does the story fit all admissible evidence or only persuasive evidence?
7. Are statistical patterns being translated responsibly into individual claims?
8. Are causal claims being made from correlational evidence?
9. Are institutional procedures improving or distorting evaluation?
10. What role do adversarial structures play in revealing or hiding evidence?
11. What role do peer review, replication, or cross-examination play?
12. Is the evidence robust across methods?
13. Does the argument confuse evidential sufficiency for one purpose with sufficiency for another?
14. How should uncertainty be communicated?
15. What does this evidence not show?

# X. Arguments from Authority and Expert Opinion

## A. Legitimate Authority Questions

1. Who is being cited as an authority?
2. What exactly does the authority claim?
3. Is the authority an expert in the relevant domain?
4. Is the domain of expertise clearly defined?
5. Does the authority’s expertise match the specific question?
6. Is the authority speaking within their area of competence?
7. Is the claim supported by the authority’s field or merely by the individual?
8. Is there expert consensus?
9. If there is disagreement, how serious and widespread is it?
10. What is the authority’s track record?
11. What methods does the authority rely on?
12. Is the authority accountable to professional standards?
13. Does the authority have conflicts of interest?
14. Is the authority being quoted accurately and in context?
15. What would be a responsible level of deference?

## B. Expert Disagreement Questions

1. What exactly do the experts disagree about?
2. Do they disagree about facts, methods, interpretations, values, predictions, or policy implications?
3. Are the experts equally qualified in the relevant domain?
4. Is one side closer to the relevant evidence?
5. Is there a majority or consensus position?
6. How reliable is the process that produced that consensus?
7. Are dissenting experts raising serious methodological concerns or merely expressing contrarian views?
8. Are lay audiences cherry-picking experts who fit prior beliefs?
9. Are media formats exaggerating disagreement?
10. Are institutional pressures shaping expert claims?
11. Is uncertainty being communicated honestly?
12. What should a non-expert believe when experts disagree?
13. What should a non-expert do when action is required before certainty is available?
14. How can deference avoid both gullibility and anti-expert cynicism?
15. What would change your assessment of the expert landscape?

## C. Fallacious Authority Questions

1. Is the appeal to authority replacing evidence rather than transmitting it?
2. Is a celebrity being treated as an expert?
3. Is expertise in one domain being transferred illegitimately to another?
4. Is a fake credential being used to create credibility?
5. Is a minority expert being presented as representative of the field?
6. Is paid or ideologically captured expertise being hidden?
7. Is the authority’s statement outdated?
8. Is the authority being cited selectively?
9. Is the authority being used to shut down legitimate questioning?
10. Is distrust of authority being used to elevate worse sources?
11. Does the argument exploit institutional prestige without methodological substance?
