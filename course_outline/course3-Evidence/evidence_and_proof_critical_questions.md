# Critical Questions for Course 3: Evidence & Proof

## Purpose of this Question Bank

This document collects critical questions aligned with the course **Evidence & Proof**. The questions are designed to help students reason carefully about what evidence is, how it supports claims, how proof works under uncertainty, and how evidential reasoning varies across law, science, history, policy, journalism, and everyday life.

The questions are not merely comprehension checks. They are meant to provoke analysis, comparison, self-correction, and responsible judgment. They can be used for seminar discussion, reading responses, exams, case analysis, argument maps, Wigmore charts, Schum-style inference networks, capstone projects, and reflective writing.

# XVII. Short Reusable Question Sets

## Five-Question Evidence Triage

1. What is the claim or hypothesis at issue?
2. What exactly is being offered as evidence?
3. Why is it relevant to that claim rather than merely interesting?
4. How strong is the support, given alternatives and background knowledge?
5. What could defeat, weaken, or reframe the evidence?

## Five-Question Source Audit

1. Who or what produced the evidence?
2. By what method was it produced?
3. What incentives, limitations, or biases might affect it?
4. Is there independent corroboration?
5. What higher-order evidence do we have about its reliability?

## Five-Question Missing Evidence Check

1. What evidence would we expect to find if the claim were true?
2. What evidence would we expect to find if the claim were false?
3. What evidence is absent?
4. Is the absence meaningful or unsurprising?
5. Was the search competent and complete enough to make absence evidential?

## Five-Question Corroboration Check

1. How many lines of evidence support the claim?
2. Are they genuinely independent?
3. Do they support the same claim or different parts of the story?
4. Do they share a common source, method, or assumption?
5. Would the conclusion survive if one line of evidence failed?

## Five-Question Story Check

1. What story is being told?
2. What evidence anchors the story?
3. What evidence does the story ignore or strain to explain?
4. What rival story fits the evidence?
5. Is narrative coherence being confused with proof?

## Five-Question Bayesian Check

1. What hypotheses are being compared?
2. How expected is the evidence if the favored hypothesis is true?
3. How expected is the evidence if a rival hypothesis is true?
4. What prior assumptions are being made?
5. Does the evidence genuinely discriminate between hypotheses?

## Five-Question Proof and Burden Check

1. Who bears the burden of proof?
2. What standard of proof applies?
3. Has the burden been met?
4. What uncertainty remains?
5. Are the consequences of error being handled responsibly?

## Five-Question Pathology Check

1. Is the evidence cherry-picked?
2. Is missing evidence being ignored?
3. Is vividness being mistaken for probative force?
4. Is precision being mistaken for rigor?
5. Are evidential standards being applied symmetrically?
