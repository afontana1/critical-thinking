# Course Overview: Evidence & Proof

**Big question:**
What *is* evidence, how does it support claims and decisions across law, science, history, and everyday life, and how should we *reason with it* responsibly under uncertainty?

## By the end, students should be able to:

* Distinguish **evidence**, **data**, **reasons**, **arguments**, **explanations**, and **proof**, and explain how they interact.
* Explain major **theories of evidence** in epistemology, including evidence as propositions, experiences, and knowledge, and see how these theories affect practical reasoning. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][1]; [Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy][8])
* Describe and evaluate major **types and sources of evidence**: perception, memory, testimony, documentary evidence, physical/trace evidence, expert evidence, statistical evidence, anecdotal evidence, negative evidence, and higher-order evidence. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][2]; [Higher-Order Evidence][5])
* Understand **Twining’s vision** of evidence as a **multi-disciplinary subject**, and explain how the study of proof connects law, science, history, and everyday reasoning. ([Oxford Academic][15]; [Cambridge University Press][19])
* Use **Wigmorean charting**, **Schum-style inference networks**, and related mapping tools to analyze evidential structure, chains of support, convergent evidence, and ancillary evidence. ([PhilPapers][3]; [Cambridge University Press][16])
* Analyze evidential reasoning using **story-based**, **scheme-based**, and **Bayesian** approaches, including **likelihoods**, **Bayes factors**, and hybrid models.
* Explain **burdens of proof**, **standards of proof**, and **presumptions** across legal, scientific, policy, and everyday contexts. ([PhilArchive][14]; [Wikipedia][12]; [Wikipedia][13])
* Assess **relevance**, **probative value**, **credibility**, **reliability**, **corroboration**, **independence**, and **defeat**. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][2]; [Oxford Academic][20])
* Critically evaluate **evidence-based practice and policy**, including debates over **hierarchies of evidence**, expert evidence, forensic evidence, and evidential pluralism. ([RSS][4]; [PhilSci Archive][7]; [SpringerLink][10])
* Diagnose and avoid major **evidential pathologies** such as cherry-picking, confirmation bias, neglect of missing evidence, narrative overfitting, overreliance on anecdote, survivorship bias, and “delusive exactness.” ([Higher-Order Evidence][5])

# Unit 1 – What Evidence Is

**Guiding questions**

* What *counts* as evidence?
* Evidence *for what*?
* Why do epistemologists, lawyers, scientists, and ordinary reasoners define evidence differently?

### Sub-units

1. **Everyday vs. technical concepts of evidence**

   * Everyday talk (“Do you have any evidence?”) versus technical concepts in epistemology, law, science, and historical reasoning.
   * Evidence as both an ordinary practical notion and a theoretical concept.

2. **Evidence as a relational notion**

   * Evidence is not just something one has; it is evidence **for or against** some claim, question, or hypothesis.
   * Context, alternatives, and goals of inquiry shape what counts as evidence.

3. **Evidence, reasons, arguments, and explanations**

   * Reasons as considerations favoring a claim.
   * Arguments as structured presentations of reasons.
   * Explanations as accounts of why something happened.
   * Why explanatory appeal and evidential support overlap but are not identical.

4. **Theories of evidence in epistemology**

   * Evidence as **propositions**, **experiences**, or **knowledge**.
   * Internalist and externalist pressures.
   * Why theories of evidence matter for practical judgment. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][1])

5. **Haack’s foundherentism**

   * The crossword-puzzle model of evidential support.
   * Against pure foundationalism and pure coherentism.
   * Mutual support, experiential input, and fit. ([Miami Law Repository][6])

**Core readings**

* SEP: **“Evidence.”** ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][1])
* IEP: **“Evidence.”** ([Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy][8])
* Susan Haack, *Evidence and Inquiry: A Pragmatist Reconstruction of Epistemology*. ([Miami Law Repository][6])
* Peter Dawid, William Twining, and Mimi Vasilaki (eds.), *Evidence, Inference and Enquiry*.

# Unit 2 – Twining and the Study of Evidence as a Multidisciplinary Subject

**Guiding questions**

* Why study evidence as its own field?
* What does Twining add to legal and philosophical study of proof?
* How can one course connect law, science, history, and everyday reasoning without collapsing them together?

### Sub-units

1. **Evidence as a multidisciplinary field**

   * Twining’s argument that evidence deserves sustained multi-disciplinary study.
   * Inferential reasoning as the common thread across domains. ([Oxford Academic][15]; [Cambridge University Press][19])

2. **The rationalist tradition**

   * The study of proof as analysis of relevance, weight, structure, and rational support.
   * Evidence scholarship beyond black-letter doctrine. ([Cambridge University Press][17])

3. **Analytic and synthetic tasks**

   * Breaking down evidential structure.
   * Marshaling, organizing, and comparing masses of evidence.
   * Why complex cases require both analysis and synthesis.

4. **The “middle ground” of evidence study**

   * Between abstract epistemology and highly specific institutional rules.
   * Why evidence requires both concepts and methods.

5. **Evidence across domains**

   * Law, science, history, journalism, and everyday reasoning as different evidential cultures with overlapping questions.

**Core readings**

* William Twining, **“Evidence as a Multi-Disciplinary Subject.”** ([Oxford Academic][15])
* William Twining, *Rethinking Evidence*. ([Cambridge University Press][18])
* William Twining, **“The Rationalist Tradition of Evidence Scholarship.”** ([Cambridge University Press][17])
* Jordi Ferrer Beltrán and Carmen Vázquez (eds.), **“Evidence as a Multidisciplinary Field.”** ([Cambridge University Press][19])

# Unit 3 – Evidence, Questions, Alternatives, and Belief Revision

**Guiding questions**

* What makes something evidence in context?
* Why is evidence always tied to alternatives?
* How should new evidence change belief?

### Sub-units

1. **Questions first**

   * Evidence relative to an issue under investigation.
   * Yes/no questions, comparative hypotheses, and open-ended inquiry.

2. **Alternative explanations and underdetermination**

   * The same evidence can fit more than one account.
   * Why responsible inquiry requires attention to rivals.

3. **Background knowledge and defeasibility**

   * Evidence never speaks entirely in a vacuum.
   * Background assumptions can strengthen, weaken, or redirect support.

4. **Belief revision**

   * How new evidence should rationally alter confidence.
   * Revision without certainty.
   * Light preparation for later Bayesian treatment.

5. **Suspension of judgment**

   * When mixed, weak, or defeated evidence warrants withholding belief.

**Core readings**

* SEP: **“Evidence.”** ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][1])
* SEP/related entries on **belief revision**, **skepticism**, and **disagreement**
* David Schum, *The Evidential Foundations of Probabilistic Reasoning*. ([PhilPapers][3])
* William Twining, relevant chapters from *Rethinking Evidence*. ([Cambridge University Press][18])

# Unit 4 – Sources of Evidence: Perception, Memory, and Testimony

**Guiding questions**

* What are the basic human sources of evidence?
* When should we trust what we see, remember, and are told?
* Why is testimony indispensable but risky?

### Sub-units

1. **Perception as evidence**

   * Observation, appearance, and conditions of uptake.
   * Seeing versus interpreting.

2. **Memory as retained and reconstructed evidence**

   * Memory as preservation and distortion.
   * Confidence, vividness, and reliability.

3. **Testimony as social evidence**

   * Testimony in everyday life, institutions, and law.
   * Direct dependence on others as an epistemic necessity.

4. **Credibility, trust, and expertise**

   * Sincerity, competence, bias, track record, and institutional role.
   * Expert testimony versus lay testimony.

5. **Social dependence and inherited error**

   * Networks of trust.
   * Why evidence can be amplified or corrupted socially.

**Core readings**

* SEP: **“Epistemological Problems of Testimony.”**
* SEP: **“Social Epistemology.”**
* C.A.J. Coady, *Testimony: A Philosophical Study*.
* Jennifer Lackey, *Learning from Words*.

# Unit 5 – Forms and Media of Evidence

**Guiding questions**

* In what forms does evidence appear?
* How do documentary, physical, digital, and demonstrative forms differ?
* How does material form affect evidential use?

### Sub-units

1. **Legal typologies as a general map**

   * Testimonial, documentary, physical/real, and demonstrative evidence. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][2])
   * Direct versus circumstantial evidence.

2. **Documentary evidence**

   * Records, contracts, archives, letters, emails, logs, photographs.
   * Provenance, authenticity, authorship, and context.

3. **Physical and trace evidence**

   * Fingerprints, fibers, residues, biological traces, tool marks.
   * Recovery, contamination, interpretation, and chain of custody.

4. **Digital evidence**

   * Logs, CCTV, geolocation, metadata, device records.
   * Apparent precision versus interpretive uncertainty.

5. **Demonstrative evidence and models**

   * Maps, diagrams, simulations, reconstructions, timelines.
   * Clarification versus assumption-smuggling.

**Core readings**

* SEP: **“The Legal Concept of Evidence.”** ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][2])
* William Twining, *Rethinking Evidence*. ([Cambridge University Press][18])
* Paul Roberts and Adrian Zuckerman, *Criminal Evidence*.
* Terence Anderson, David Schum, and William Twining, *Analysis of Evidence*. ([Cambridge University Press][16])

# Unit 6 – Special Kinds of Evidence

**Guiding questions**

* Are anecdotes ever good evidence?
* What is distinctive about statistical, negative, and higher-order evidence?
* What is evidence about our evidence?

### Sub-units

1. **Anecdotal evidence**

   * Its rhetorical force and accessibility.
   * When anecdotes are genuinely revealing and when they mislead.

2. **Statistical evidence**

   * Aggregate support, frequencies, distributions, and generalized patterns.
   * Tension between individualized proof and population-level support.

3. **Negative evidence and arguments from silence**

   * Evidence of absence versus absence of evidence.
   * Missing traces, missing records, and expected silence.

4. **Higher-order evidence**

   * Evidence about source quality, method quality, one’s own reliability, or the inquiry situation. ([Higher-Order Evidence][5])
   * Why higher-order evidence can rationally shift confidence.

5. **Defeaters**

   * Rebutting versus undercutting defeaters.
   * Evidence that attacks a claim versus evidence that attacks the support relation.

**Core readings**

* SEP: **“Higher-Order Evidence.”** ([Higher-Order Evidence][5])
* Douglas Walton, *Arguments from Ignorance*.
* David Schum, *The Evidential Foundations of Probabilistic Reasoning*. ([PhilPapers][3])
* Essays by Jennifer Lackey and David Christensen on defeat and higher-order evidence.

# Unit 7 – Relevance, Strength, and Probative Value

**Guiding questions**

* What makes evidence relevant?
* How strong can evidence be without being decisive?
* How do we distinguish probative force from emotional force?

### Sub-units

1. **Relevance**

   * Logical, explanatory, probabilistic, and legal relevance.
   * “Any tendency” to make a fact more or less probable. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][2])

2. **Probative value**

   * Weight, support, and evidential increment.
   * Weak evidence, cumulative force, and tipping points.

3. **Prejudice and distraction**

   * Relevant but unfairly prejudicial evidence.
   * Vividness, moral contamination, and attention capture.

4. **Source and method reliability**

   * Reliability as one contributor to evidential force.
   * Distinguishing trustworthiness from support.

5. **What is evidence law for?**

   * Connecting evidential relevance and probative force to institutional goals.
   * Truth-seeking, fairness, error management, and procedural design. ([Oxford Academic][20])

**Core readings**

* SEP: **“The Legal Concept of Evidence.”** ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][2])
* Notes to SEP **“The Legal Concept of Evidence.”** ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][11])
* Alex Stein, **“Evidence Law: What is it For?”** ([Oxford Academic][20])
* Royal Statistical Society, **Law Guides 1–4**. ([RSS][4])

# Unit 8 – Corroboration, Convergence, and the Variety of Evidence

**Guiding questions**

* Why does corroboration matter?
* When do multiple pieces of evidence genuinely count as independent?
* Is diverse evidence always stronger evidence?

### Sub-units

1. **Corroboration**

   * Mutual support across different lines of evidence.
   * Why convergence matters intuitively.

2. **Independence and pseudo-corroboration**

   * Repetition versus genuine independent support.
   * Common-source errors and hidden dependence.

3. **The variety-of-evidence thesis**

   * Diverse and independent evidence as potentially more confirmatory than repeated evidence of one kind. ([PhilSci Archive][7])
   * Intuitive and formal versions of the thesis.

4. **When variety misleads**

   * Different methods can fail together.
   * Apparent diversity may conceal common assumptions.

5. **Triangulation and robustness**

   * Mixed support across methods, perspectives, and evidential pathways.

**Core readings**

* Barbara Osimani and Jürgen Landes, **“Varieties of Error and Varieties of Evidence in Scientific Inference.”** ([PhilSci Archive][7])
* Published version / related discussion of the same work. ([journals.uchicago.edu][9])
* Work on the **variety of evidence thesis and independence**. ([SpringerLink][10])
* William Twining, *Rethinking Evidence*. ([Cambridge University Press][18])

# Unit 9 – Wigmore and the Analysis of Evidential Structure

**Guiding questions**

* Why map evidence instead of just discussing it?
* What does Wigmorean analysis make visible?
* How do chains, convergences, and ancillary evidence work?

### Sub-units

1. **Why evidential structure needs explicit representation**

   * Complex evidential bodies exceed unaided intuition.
   * The need to display inferential structure clearly.

2. **Wigmore’s charting method**

   * Ultimate probanda, interim probanda, and evidential propositions.
   * Arrows of support, chains of inference, and convergent support.

3. **Ancillary evidence**

   * Evidence about the quality or credibility of other evidence.
   * Support for support.

4. **Catenated and convergent evidence**

   * Linked chains versus multiple converging supports.
   * Distinct vulnerabilities in each.

5. **Wigmore as analytic pillar**

   * Charting as disciplined evidential analysis rather than mere visualization.
   * The lasting relevance of Wigmore’s method for modern proof analysis.

**Core readings**

* John Henry Wigmore, selections from *The Science of Judicial Proof* or *The Principles of Judicial Proof*.
* Terence Anderson, David Schum, and William Twining, *Analysis of Evidence*. ([Cambridge University Press][16])
* Notes to SEP **“The Legal Concept of Evidence.”** for Wigmore-related background. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][11])
* William Twining, discussions of Wigmore in *Rethinking Evidence*. ([Cambridge University Press][18])

**Guiding questions**

* How does Schum extend the Wigmorean project?
* What do inference networks add?
* How do modern evidential maps prepare the ground for probabilistic analysis?

### Sub-units

1. **From Wigmore to Schum**

   * Continuities and extensions.
   * Why Schum modernizes rather than replaces Wigmore.

2. **Inference networks**

   * Nodes, links, hypotheses, sub-hypotheses, and evidence chains.
   * Mapping structure, dependence, and vulnerability.

3. **Ancillary and meta-evidence in network form**

   * Reliability, credibility, and evidential quality as explicit nodes.

4. **Masses of evidence**

   * Organizing large, heterogeneous evidential bodies.
   * Why structured analysis matters in real cases.

5. **Networks as bridge tools**

   * A bridge from qualitative mapping to probabilistic and hybrid models.

**Core readings**

* David Schum, *The Evidential Foundations of Probabilistic Reasoning*. ([PhilPapers][3])
* Terence Anderson, David Schum, and William Twining, *Analysis of Evidence*. ([Cambridge University Press][16])

# Unit 11 – Stories, Bayes, and Hybrid Models

**Guiding questions**

* How do stories and probabilities each contribute to evidential reasoning?
* What do Bayes factors add to conceptual understanding?
* Are narrative and probabilistic models rivals or partners?

### Sub-units

1. **Story-based reasoning**

   * Constructing and comparing narratives that account for the evidence.
   * Coherence, completeness, and fit.

2. **Anchored narratives**

   * Stories constrained by evidence and background knowledge.
   * Narrative as structured, not free-floating.

3. **Bayesian confirmation**

   * Evidence as changing comparative plausibility.
   * Likelihoods, priors, posteriors, and updating at a conceptual level.

4. **Bayes factors and evidential weight**

   * Bayes factor as a measure of how much more expected evidence is on one hypothesis than another.
   * Weight of evidence without turning the course into a statistics course.

5. **Hybrid theories**

   * Stories, arguments, networks, and probabilities together.
   * Why mixed models are attractive in legal and forensic reasoning.

**Core readings**

* Floris Bex, *Arguments, Stories and Criminal Evidence*.
* Floris Bex, work on **hybrid theories of stories and arguments**.
* David Schum, *The Evidential Foundations of Probabilistic Reasoning*. ([PhilPapers][3])
* J.N. Morey, J.-W. Romeijn, and J.N. Rouder, **“The philosophy of Bayes factors and the quantification of statistical evidence.”**
* Paolo Garbolino and related work on Bayesian approaches to scientific and legal evidence.

# Unit 13 – Evidence Across Domains, Hierarchies, and Evidential Pathologies

**Guiding questions**

* How do different domains organize evidence differently?
* What should we make of hierarchies of evidence?
* How does evidential reasoning go wrong?

### Sub-units

1. **Evidence across domains**

   * Law, science, history, journalism, public controversy, and policy.
   * Shared inferential problems under different institutional constraints.
   * Return to Twining’s multidisciplinary framing. ([Oxford Academic][15]; [Cambridge University Press][19])

2. **Hierarchies of evidence**

   * Why evidence hierarchies arose in medicine and policy.
   * Their strengths: anti-bias safeguards, procedural discipline, and standardization.
   * Their limits: context-insensitivity, underweighting mechanisms, testimony, and mixed evidential support. ([RSS][4])

3. **Expert evidence and evidential pluralism**

   * Why no single evidential model captures every serious inquiry.
   * Statistical, mechanistic, testimonial, documentary, and experiential support in combination.

4. **Evidential pathologies**

   * Cherry-picking, confirmation bias, neglect of missing evidence, narrative overfitting, survivorship bias, and motivated skepticism.

5. **Delusive exactness and responsible inquiry**

   * Haack’s warning against pseudo-rigor.
   * Seeking disconfirmation, tracking alternatives, and recognizing when suspension of judgment is rational.

**Core readings**

* William Twining, *Rethinking Evidence*. ([Cambridge University Press][18])
* Barbara Osimani and related work on evidence pluralism and variety. ([PhilSci Archive][7]; [journals.uchicago.edu][9]; [SpringerLink][10])
* Susan Haack, **“Proof, Probability, and Statistics: The Problem of ‘Delusive Exactness’.”**
* Jennifer Mnookin and related work on forensic epistemology.
* Royal Statistical Society, **Law Guides 1–4**. ([RSS][4])

## Possible capstone activities (for the Evidence & Proof section)

1. **Wigmore–Schum case analysis**

   * Students choose a real or fictional case involving conflicting testimony, documentary evidence, traces, expert evidence, or statistical support.
   * Tasks:

     * build a **Wigmore chart** or **Schum-style inference network**;
     * identify ancillary evidence;
     * distinguish convergent from chained support;
     * evaluate where the structure is weak or vulnerable.

2. **Comparative evidential reasoning project**

   * Students take one controversy and analyze it in multiple frames:

     * legal,
     * scientific/policy,
     * everyday/public reasoning.

   * Tasks:

     * classify the evidence;
     * identify burdens and standards;
     * compare what counts as adequate support in each domain;
     * assess whether hierarchy-based or pluralist approaches fit best.

3. **Stories vs. Bayes reflective memo**

   * Students compare a story-based analysis with a conceptual Bayesian analysis of the same body of evidence.
   * They must explain:

     * what each model highlights,
     * what each suppresses,
     * and whether a hybrid model gives the best account.
