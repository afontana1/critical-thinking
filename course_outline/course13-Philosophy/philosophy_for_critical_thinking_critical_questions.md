# Critical Questions for Philosophy for Critical Thinking

## Purpose of this Question Bank

These questions are designed to support the course **Philosophy for Critical Thinking — Concepts, Distinctions, and Worldview Analysis**. They are meant to provoke conceptual discipline, interpretive charity, argument reconstruction, worldview analysis, and reflective judgment.

They can be used for seminars, writing prompts, exams, debate preparation, capstone scaffolding, journal reflections, and cross-course synthesis.

---

## 1. Philosophy as Critical Thinking

### Philosophy as Second-Order Inquiry

1. What makes a question philosophical rather than merely factual, scientific, historical, religious, or rhetorical?
2. When does a disagreement require more evidence, and when does it require clearer concepts?
3. What assumptions about knowledge, reality, value, or language are hidden inside this argument?
4. What would it mean to answer this question at a deeper level rather than simply state an opinion?
5. How does philosophy help us examine the standards by which we judge claims?
6. When does clarification solve a problem, and when does it only reveal a deeper disagreement?
7. What is the difference between asking, “Is this true?” and asking, “What would make this true?”
8. How does philosophy help us avoid treating inherited categories as obvious or natural?
9. What intellectual virtues are required for philosophical inquiry?
10. What vices make philosophical discussion collapse into wordplay, ideology, or mere opinion?

### Philosophical Method

1. What concept is being analyzed, and why does it matter?
2. What distinction would clarify this dispute?
3. What thought experiment would test the principle being used?
4. What counterexample would challenge the proposed definition?
5. What would follow if this claim were taken to its logical conclusion?
6. Is this argument best evaluated by reductio, analogy, counterexample, conceptual analysis, or reflective equilibrium?
7. What is the strongest charitable version of the opposing view?
8. What assumption must be granted before this argument becomes plausible?
9. Does this argument prove its conclusion, or only make it more reasonable?
10. What would count as philosophical progress in this discussion?

## 2. Conceptual Distinctions and Clear Thinking

### Foundational Distinctions

1. Is this claim descriptive, normative, conceptual, empirical, interpretive, or metaphysical?
2. Is the argument confusing facts with values?
3. Is the argument moving from “is” to “ought” without a bridging principle?
4. Is the claim known, if at all, a priori or a posteriori?
5. Is the claim analytic, synthetic, or does that distinction itself break down here?
6. Is the dispute about appearance, reality, or the relation between them?
7. Is the concept being used objectively, subjectively, or intersubjectively?
8. Is the speaker treating a practical convention as if it were a metaphysical fact?
9. Is the argument confusing what is necessary with what is sufficient?
10. What changes if we distinguish between a condition, a cause, a reason, and an explanation?

### Classification and Category Discipline

1. What category does this thing belong to, and who decides?
2. Is this a type, a token, a case, an instance, a member, or an example?
3. Are we dealing with an essential property or an accidental feature?
4. What edge cases test the boundaries of this concept?
5. Does this definition capture ordinary usage, theoretical purpose, or normative aspiration?
6. Is this a genuine disagreement or a pseudo-dispute caused by shifting meanings?
7. What would be lost if this distinction were erased?
8. What would be gained if this distinction were revised?
9. Does this argument depend on a category mistake?
10. Is the classification tracking reality, serving a purpose, or enforcing a social norm?

### Vagueness, Ambiguity, and Borderline Cases

1. Is the term vague, ambiguous, contested, or merely unfamiliar?
2. Does the argument exploit a borderline case?
3. Is there a sorites-style problem hiding in this dispute?
4. When does gradual change become a change in kind?
5. Does the concept have sharp boundaries, family resemblances, or context-sensitive application?
6. What practical consequences follow from drawing the boundary here rather than there?
7. Who benefits from keeping this concept vague?
8. Who benefits from making it artificially precise?
9. Is conceptual precision improving inquiry or creating false clarity?
10. What would a better concept need to accomplish?

## 3. Knowledge, Justification, and Error

### Knowledge and Justification

1. What would it take to know this rather than merely believe it?
2. Is the belief true by accident, or is it connected to truth in the right way?
3. What does this case reveal about the limits of the justified-true-belief model?
4. What defeaters would undermine this belief?
5. Is the justification internal to the thinker’s perspective, external in the reliability of the process, or both?
6. Is the person justified even if they cannot fully explain their justification?
7. Can someone know something for bad reasons?
8. Can someone fail to know something even if they are accidentally correct?
9. Is the argument asking for certainty where knowledge only requires adequate justification?
10. How should intellectual humility shape claims of knowledge?

### Competing Theories of Justification

1. Is this belief supported by foundations, coherence, evidence, reliability, or social trust?
2. What are the stopping points in this chain of justification?
3. Does the argument rely on self-evident beliefs?
4. Does coherence among beliefs make them more likely to be true?
5. Can a reliable process justify belief even if the believer does not understand the process?
6. What would an evidentialist require before accepting this claim?
7. What would a reliabilist ask about the method that produced the belief?
8. What would a Bayesian ask about prior probabilities and updating?
9. What does social epistemology add that individual epistemology misses?
10. Which theory of justification best fits ordinary critical thinking?

### Skepticism and Fallibilism

1. Is skepticism being used as a tool for inquiry or as a weapon against inquiry?
2. What skeptical challenge is being raised: global, local, methodological, or motivated?
3. Does this skeptical demand set an impossible standard?
4. Is the argument vulnerable to the Münchhausen trilemma?
5. Where does the chain of reasons stop, and is that stopping point legitimate?
6. Is the skeptic demanding certainty in a domain where fallible knowledge is enough?
7. What is the difference between “possibly false” and “unjustified”?
8. How should a fallibilist act when certainty is unavailable?
9. When does skepticism become cynicism?
10. What intellectual virtues make skepticism healthy?

## 5. Metaphysics and Ontology

### Ontological Commitment

1. What kinds of things does this argument assume exist?
2. Is the argument committed to objects, properties, relations, events, processes, states of affairs, or institutions?
3. Are the relevant entities concrete, abstract, social, fictional, or theoretical?
4. Is the argument treating a model, category, or construct as if it were an object?
5. What would an ontological audit of this worldview reveal?
6. Are we discovering a structure in reality or imposing a classificatory scheme?
7. What is the difference between naming something and showing that it exists?
8. Does the argument depend on a hidden ontology of persons, groups, markets, minds, or values?
9. What would change if we treated this phenomenon as a process rather than a substance?
10. How do ontological assumptions shape practical decisions?

### Universals, Kinds, and Essence

1. Are categories real features of the world or human-made groupings?
2. Is this an argument for realism, nominalism, or some intermediate position?
3. Does the category pick out a natural kind, a social kind, or a practical convenience?
4. What makes two things instances of the same kind?
5. Is the argument relying on essential properties?
6. What happens if the supposed essence is historically contingent?
7. Are social categories less real because they are socially constructed?
8. Can a constructed category have real causal power?
9. What does the Ship of Theseus problem teach about identity through change?
10. When does classification illuminate reality, and when does it trap thought?

### Modality and Possibility

1. Is the claim about what is actual, possible, necessary, contingent, or impossible?
2. What possible worlds or counterfactual scenarios are being assumed?
3. Is the speaker treating a contingent arrangement as necessary?
4. Is the speaker treating a merely possible scenario as practically relevant?
5. What would have to be different for this claim to be false?
6. Are the relevant possibilities logical, metaphysical, physical, social, or practical?
7. How does modal thinking help clarify political, ethical, or scientific arguments?
8. What possibilities does this worldview make visible or invisible?
9. What is the difference between imagining something and showing that it is possible?
10. How should critical thinkers reason about necessity claims?

## 6. Meaning, Language, and Interpretation

### Meaning and Reference

1. How does this word connect to what it refers to?
2. Is the dispute about reference, sense, use, connotation, or implication?
3. Does the argument assume that words have fixed meanings independent of practice?
4. Is the speaker relying on ordinary language, technical usage, or rhetorical redefinition?
5. What happens if the same term has different meanings in different language games?
6. Is the argument confusing a word with the thing it names?
7. Is the definition descriptive, stipulative, theoretical, persuasive, or revisionary?
8. What would a semantic analysis reveal?
9. What would a pragmatic analysis reveal?
10. How does meaning shift across contexts, audiences, and purposes?

### Pragmatics and Communication

1. What is said, what is implied, and what is left unsaid?
2. Is the speaker exploiting implicature for plausible deniability?
3. Which conversational maxim is being followed, flouted, or manipulated?
4. What context is needed to understand the utterance?
5. Is the meaning dependent on deixis, timing, shared background, or social position?
6. Does the statement function as assertion, command, promise, threat, joke, signal, or performance?
7. What are the felicity conditions for this speech act?
8. Is relevance being guided or manipulated?
9. Does the argument depend on ambiguity between literal and speaker meaning?
10. How does pragmatic awareness improve critical listening?

### Interpretation and Hermeneutics

1. What is the text, action, or artifact being interpreted?
2. What whole gives meaning to this part?
3. What parts challenge the interpretation of the whole?
4. What role do tradition, genre, audience, and historical context play?
5. Is the interpretation charitable without being gullible?
6. Is the interpretation suspicious without being paranoid?
7. What does this interpretation reveal, and what does it obscure?
8. Are we interpreting meaning, intention, effect, or social function?
9. When do multiple interpretations remain legitimate?
10. How should critical thinkers handle interpretive disagreement?
