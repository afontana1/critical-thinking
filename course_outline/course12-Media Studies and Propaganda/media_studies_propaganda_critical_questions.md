# Critical Questions for Media Studies, Propaganda & Critical Thinking

## Purpose of this question bank

These questions are designed to help students analyze how media ecosystems shape attention, belief, identity, trust, and action. They are aligned with the course goals of distinguishing persuasion from manipulation, diagnosing propaganda and disinformation, evaluating testimony and expertise, practicing verification, and cultivating calibrated epistemic agency in manipulated information environments.

The questions can be used for discussion, reading guides, written reflections, case analysis, capstone projects, debate preparation, media audits, and verification labs.

---

## 1. Media Ecosystems, Incentives, and Attention

### Information ecosystems

1. What actors make up the information ecosystem around this claim: journalists, platforms, influencers, audiences, institutions, advertisers, activists, states, or opportunists?
2. What incentives shape what gets produced, amplified, ignored, or distorted?
3. Who benefits if this narrative spreads?
4. Who bears the cost if this narrative spreads?
5. What does this ecosystem reward: accuracy, speed, outrage, loyalty, novelty, fear, identity performance, or status?
6. What kinds of claims are structurally advantaged in this media environment?
7. What kinds of claims are structurally disadvantaged, even if they are true?
8. How does the medium shape the message: headline, short video, meme, podcast, thread, livestream, op-ed, dashboard, or official report?
9. What is the difference between asking “Is this claim true?” and asking “Why did this claim travel so far?”
10. What would an ecosystem map reveal that a simple fact-check would miss?

### Attention and virality

11. Why did this claim attract attention?
12. What emotional hooks are built into it?
13. Does the content invite fear, disgust, resentment, amusement, superiority, urgency, or belonging?
14. How does the platform’s ranking or recommendation structure shape what people notice?
15. Is the claim optimized for understanding or for sharing?
16. Does the claim become more persuasive because it is repeated?
17. What is the difference between a claim being visible and a claim being credible?
18. How does salience become mistaken for importance?
19. How does virality become mistaken for consensus?
20. What would change if the same information were presented slowly, without outrage cues or social metrics?

### Trust and legitimacy

21. What sources of legitimacy does this message borrow: journalism, science, patriotism, religion, expertise, populism, lived experience, or “common sense”?
22. Is trust being earned, borrowed, faked, or weaponized?
23. What institutions are being asked to lose credibility?
24. What substitute authorities are being offered?
25. Does the message repair trust, redirect trust, or destroy trust indiscriminately?
26. What would calibrated trust look like in this case?
27. Is distrust being used as a tool of inquiry or as an identity stance?
28. When does skepticism become a gateway to manipulation?

62. When does emotional appeal become manipulation?
63. When does simplification become distortion?
64. When does repetition become indoctrination?
65. When does advocacy become propaganda?
66. Is the audience being invited to reason or recruited to perform loyalty?
67. Are people being treated as citizens, consumers, targets, followers, or weapons?

### White, gray, and black propaganda

### Ambiguity and plausible deniability

85. What terms are strategically vague?

87. What can the speaker deny having said?
88. Does the message rely on insinuation, dog whistles, suggestive questions, or selective quotation?
89. Is there motte-and-bailey movement between a defensible claim and a stronger inflammatory claim?
90. Is ambiguity being used to expand the audience while avoiding accountability?
91. What would the argument look like if all implied claims were made explicit?

### Fallacies and distortions

92. Is this a straw man, ad hominem, false dilemma, slippery slope, appeal to fear, appeal to popularity, or appeal to fake authority?
93. Is the fallacy a simple mistake or a strategic rhetorical move?
94. What legitimate argument scheme is being misused?
95. Does the claim rely on a vivid anecdote where representative evidence is needed?
96. Does it treat absence of evidence as evidence of absence without a competent search?
97. Does it apply strict standards to opponents and lax standards to allies?
98. How could the argument be repaired into a more responsible form?

## 19. Short Reusable Question Sets

### The five-question media triage

1. What exactly is being claimed?
2. Who is behind it, and what do they want?
3. What evidence is offered, and what evidence is missing?
4. How is the message trying to make me feel?
5. What should I do before believing, sharing, or acting?

### The propaganda diagnosis checklist

1. What enemy, crisis, or grievance is constructed?
2. What identity does the message offer the audience?
3. What emotions are activated?
4. What evidence standards are used for allies versus opponents?
5. What action or attitude is made to seem necessary?
6. What is hidden, laundered, exaggerated, or dehumanized?

### The participatory disinformation checklist

1. How are audiences invited to help?
2. What roles are assigned?
3. What rewards sustain participation?
4. How does the narrative adapt to new events?
5. How does it protect itself from correction?
6. What would make exit possible?

### The expert-check workflow

1. What domain of expertise is required?
2. Is this person qualified in that domain?
3. What does the relevant expert community think?
4. What conflicts or incentives matter?
5. What primary sources support the claim?
6. How confident should a non-expert be?

### The countermeasure design checklist

1. What harm are we trying to reduce?
2. Who is the audience?
3. What tactic should be prebunked or debunked?
4. How can we avoid amplifying the falsehood?
5. What response preserves autonomy and dignity?
6. What unintended consequences should we monitor?
