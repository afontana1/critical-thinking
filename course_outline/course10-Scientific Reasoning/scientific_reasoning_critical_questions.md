# Critical Questions for Course 10: Scientific Reasoning

## How to Use This Question Bank

These questions are designed to support discussion, written reflection, paper analysis, exams, research critique, and capstone work for **Scientific Reasoning – From Hypotheses to Institutions**. They are not merely comprehension questions. They are meant to train students to ask how science reasons: how it frames questions, uses evidence, builds models, evaluates theories, handles uncertainty, corrects error, and depends on institutions.

The central aim is to help students neither worship nor dismiss science, but analyze scientific claims as structured, fallible, socially organized attempts to learn about the world.

---

# I. Foundational Questions About Scientific Reasoning

## A. What Makes Reasoning Scientific?

1. What distinguishes scientific reasoning from ordinary everyday reasoning?
2. Is science defined by a single method, a family of methods, or a set of institutional norms?
3. When someone says “science shows,” what exactly should we ask next?
4. What makes a claim scientific rather than merely technical, mathematical, or “science-sounding”?
5. Can a claim be scientific even if it later turns out to be false?
6. Can a claim be true but not scientifically established?
7. What is the difference between scientific knowledge and expert opinion?
8. What is the difference between scientific inquiry and scientific rhetoric?
9. What makes scientific reasoning more reliable than unaided intuition in some domains?
10. What limits remain even when reasoning is scientific?

## B. Science as Structured Inquiry

1. What question is the scientific inquiry trying to answer?
2. Is the question descriptive, causal, mechanistic, predictive, explanatory, or evaluative?
3. What observations, measurements, or data are being treated as relevant?
4. What assumptions connect the observations to the claim?
5. What alternatives were considered and ruled out?
6. What would count as evidence against the claim?
7. How does the inquiry move from observation to hypothesis, from hypothesis to test, and from test to revision?
8. Where does judgment enter the process despite the use of formal methods?
9. What parts of the reasoning are explicit, and what parts are hidden in background assumptions?
10. What makes this inquiry self-correcting, if it is?

## C. Science, Non-Science, and Pseudoscience

1. What is the difference between a weak scientific claim and a pseudoscientific claim?
2. Does the claim make risky predictions, or does it reinterpret every possible outcome as confirmation?
3. Are failed predictions treated as reasons to revise the claim, or are they explained away with ad hoc rescue moves?
4. Does the claim rely on selective evidence, testimonial authority, or isolated anecdotes?
5. Are critics engaged seriously, or dismissed as biased, closed-minded, or part of a conspiracy?
6. Are the methods transparent enough that others could check them?
7. Does the claim imitate the surface features of science without adopting scientific discipline?
8. What role do confirmation bias, motivated reasoning, and illusory correlation play in the claim’s appeal?
9. Does the claim become more precise over time, or more evasive?
10. What would responsible skepticism require in this case?

# II. Questions About Scientific Method and Scientific Methods

## A. Against the Simplistic “Scientific Method”

1. What is misleading about presenting science as a single linear sequence of steps?
2. How do real scientific investigations depart from textbook diagrams of the scientific method?
3. When does inquiry begin with theory rather than observation?
4. When does inquiry begin with anomaly, puzzle, crisis, or practical need?
5. How do different sciences use different methods while still counting as scientific?
6. Why might methods appropriate in physics fail in social science, medicine, or ecology?
7. What is gained by simplifying scientific method for teaching, and what is lost?
8. How do iteration, revision, replication, and critique fit into the scientific process?
9. What does a scientific method make visible, and what might it obscure?
10. How should students revise the phrase “the scientific method” after studying real scientific practice?

## B. Research Process Questions

1. What is the research question, and how precisely is it framed?
2. What design is used to address the question?
3. What data are collected, and why are those data appropriate?
4. What variables are measured, and how are they operationalized?
5. What assumptions make the analysis meaningful?
6. What statistical, causal, mechanistic, or interpretive reasoning is used?
7. How are uncertainty and error handled?
8. What conclusions are warranted by the design?
9. What conclusions would go beyond the design?
10. What further research would strengthen, weaken, or refine the result?

## C. Cross-Disciplinary Method Questions

1. How do natural sciences, social sciences, and formal sciences differ in their methods of justification?
2. How does measurement differ across physical, biological, psychological, and social domains?
3. How does control differ in laboratory, field, historical, and computational research?
4. What kinds of evidence are privileged in this field, and why?
5. What counts as replication in this field?
6. What counts as explanation in this field?
7. How does the field handle complexity, context, and uncertainty?
8. Does the field rely more on experiments, observation, simulation, case comparison, or modeling?
9. What institutional norms shape method choice in this discipline?
10. How might another discipline criticize or supplement this field’s reasoning?

# III. Questions About Forms of Scientific Inference

## A. Deduction

1. What deductive inferences appear in the scientific argument?
2. Are predictions deduced from a theory, model, or hypothesis?
3. Are the premises strong enough to support the conclusion if taken as true?
4. Does the argument confuse logical validity with empirical truth?
5. Are hidden auxiliary assumptions needed to derive the prediction?
6. If the prediction fails, does that refute the theory, an auxiliary assumption, a measurement procedure, or a model specification?
7. How does deductive structure help clarify a scientific test?
8. Where does deduction create an appearance of certainty that the empirical situation does not support?
9. What is the difference between a valid inference and a well-confirmed scientific claim?
10. How does deduction interact with observation and experimentation?

## B. Induction

1. What generalization is being drawn from observed cases?
2. How representative are the observed cases?
3. What is the relevant population or domain of generalization?
4. How many cases are enough, and enough for what purpose?
5. Are the observations independent, diverse, and robust?
6. What unobserved cases might challenge the generalization?
7. Is the inductive inference strengthened by variety of evidence?
8. Is the argument vulnerable to selection bias, survivorship bias, or publication bias?
9. What background assumptions make the induction plausible?
10. How should confidence change as more evidence accumulates?

## C. Abduction and Inference to the Best Explanation

1. What phenomenon needs explaining?
2. What candidate explanations are being considered?
3. Why is one explanation judged better than the others?
4. Are there unconceived alternatives that have not been considered?
5. Does the favored explanation explain more than the data it was designed to fit?
6. Is the explanation simple because it is genuinely elegant, or because it ignores complexity?
7. Does the explanation cohere with background knowledge?
8. Does the explanation generate new testable expectations?
9. Is the inference to the best explanation being confused with proof?
10. What evidence would distinguish between the leading explanations?

## D. Mechanistic Reasoning

1. What mechanism is claimed to connect cause and effect?
2. What are the entities, activities, and organization involved in the mechanism?
3. Is the mechanism directly observed, inferred, modeled, or merely speculated?
4. Does the mechanism explain how the effect is produced, or merely redescribe the association?
5. Are there missing links in the proposed causal chain?
6. Does the mechanism operate at the right level of analysis?
7. Does mechanism evidence strengthen external validity or transportability?
8. Can the same effect arise through different mechanisms?
9. Could the mechanism operate in one context but fail in another?
10. When is mechanism evidence necessary, and when is it less important?

# IV. Questions About Theories, Models, and Observation

## A. Theories

1. What theory is being used to interpret the evidence?
2. Is the theory a broad framework, a narrow hypothesis, or a specific model?
3. What phenomena does the theory explain well?
4. What phenomena does the theory struggle to explain?
5. What assumptions does the theory make about the world?
6. What concepts does the theory introduce or redefine?
7. How does the theory guide what researchers look for?
8. How does the theory shape what counts as evidence?
9. What rival theories could explain the same evidence?
10. Is the theory being used as a tool for inquiry or as a shield against criticism?

## B. Theory-Ladenness of Observation

1. What background concepts make the observation possible?
2. What instruments, categories, or operational definitions shape what is observed?
3. What would a researcher from another theoretical background notice differently?
4. Are the data treated as raw facts when they are actually processed through assumptions?
5. How do measurement choices embody theoretical commitments?
6. What is invisible because of the current conceptual scheme?
7. How might changing the theory change the interpretation of the same observation?
8. Does theory-ladenness undermine objectivity, or does it show why objectivity requires discipline?
9. How can transparency about assumptions improve scientific objectivity?
10. How can multiple perspectives help correct theory-driven blindness?

## C. Models in Science

1. What kind of model is being used: statistical, mechanistic, computational, mathematical, conceptual, or physical?
2. What is the model for: explanation, prediction, exploration, design, or communication?
3. What does the model represent, simplify, or idealize?
4. What is left out of the model?
5. What assumptions make the model tractable?
6. What assumptions make the model fragile?
7. How is the model connected to data?
8. How is the model calibrated, validated, or compared with reality?
9. What is the model’s domain of applicability?
10. Is the model being treated as an aid to reasoning or as an oracle?

## D. Theoretical Virtues

1. How empirically adequate is the theory?
2. How simple is the theory, and is that simplicity illuminating or distorting?
3. Is the theory internally consistent?
4. Is the theory externally consistent with established knowledge?
5. How much explanatory power does the theory have?
6. Does the theory unify diverse phenomena?
7. Is the theory fertile: does it generate new questions, predictions, or research programs?
8. What trade-offs exist among simplicity, realism, precision, and scope?
9. Are theoretical virtues being applied consistently across competing theories?
10. When does theory revision become ad hoc rescue?

# V. Questions About Scientific Explanation

## A. What Counts as Explanation?

1. What kind of why-question is being asked?
2. Is the explanation causal, mechanistic, statistical, functional, historical, or unifying?
3. Does the explanation show why the phenomenon occurred, how it occurred, or what would happen under intervention?
4. Does the explanation merely fit the facts after the fact, or does it guide new inquiry?
5. What contrast class is implied: why this rather than what alternative?
6. Does the explanation identify a difference-maker?
7. Does it identify a mechanism?
8. Does it identify a lawlike regularity or pattern?
9. Does the explanation work at the right scale?
10. What would make the explanation deeper or more complete?

## B. Explanatory Depth and Limits

1. Does the explanation stop too early?
2. Does it rely on a black box?
3. Does it mistake naming a process for explaining it?
4. Does it overstate certainty in a complex system?
5. Does it ignore feedback, adaptation, or context?
6. Does it explain the average case while hiding heterogeneity?
7. Does it explain one case while failing to generalize?
8. Does it give a plausible story without adequate evidence?
9. Does it integrate evidence from multiple levels?
10. What would count as a rival explanation with equal or greater depth?

## C. Explanation Across Fields

1. How does explanation in physics differ from explanation in biology?
2. How does explanation in biology differ from explanation in social science?
3. When are laws central to explanation?
4. When are mechanisms central?
5. When are models central?
6. When are narratives or historical sequences legitimate explanatory tools?
7. How do complex systems challenge simple explanatory forms?
8. How do social meanings and institutions complicate explanation in social science?
9. Can a statistical model explain, or only predict?
10. What kind of explanation is appropriate for this domain?

# VI. Questions About Research Design and Methodology

## A. Research Design

1. What design is used, and why was it chosen?
2. What question can this design answer well?
3. What question can this design not answer?
4. What comparison is central to the design?
5. Is the design experimental, observational, quasi-experimental, qualitative, computational, or mixed-method?
6. How does the design handle confounding or alternative explanations?
7. How does the design handle measurement error?
8. How does the design handle sampling and generalization?
9. What assumptions are required for the design to support the conclusion?
10. What design would provide stronger evidence, and why might it be impractical?

## B. Experiments and Quasi-Experiments

1. What is manipulated, and what is measured?
2. Is randomization used, and what does it accomplish?
3. Is there a control or comparison group?
4. Are participants, researchers, or evaluators blinded where appropriate?
5. What threats to internal validity remain?
6. What is the relationship between experimental control and real-world realism?
7. Does the experiment support a causal claim?
8. Does the effect generalize beyond the experimental setting?
9. Are there spillovers, noncompliance, attrition, or demand effects?
10. How would replication strengthen or complicate the result?

## C. Observational and Field Research

1. What naturally occurring variation is being used?
2. How are cases, participants, or observations selected?
3. What confounders might explain the observed association?
4. What background assumptions make the observational inference plausible?
5. Is the analysis transparent about limitations?
6. Are robustness checks or sensitivity analyses reported?
7. Is the study exploratory, confirmatory, or both?
8. Does the study overclaim causality?
9. How does context shape interpretation?
10. What additional evidence would improve confidence?

## D. Qualitative and Mixed Methods

1. What kind of understanding is the qualitative evidence meant to provide?
2. How are cases selected?
3. What counts as evidence in the qualitative analysis?
4. How are interpretations checked against rival interpretations?
5. Does the study provide mechanism evidence, context, meaning, or process evidence?
6. How does qualitative evidence complement quantitative evidence?
7. Are thick description and theoretical inference clearly distinguished?
8. What is the role of researcher judgment?
9. How is credibility established?
10. What would triangulation look like in this study?

## E. Methodology as Second-Order Reflection

1. What method is being used?
2. What methodological justification is offered for using it?
3. What trade-offs does this method involve?
4. What kinds of error does the method control well?
5. What kinds of error does it leave open?
6. What assumptions about knowledge, evidence, and explanation are built into the method?
7. How might another method reveal something this one misses?
8. Are methods being chosen because they fit the question or because they are prestigious?
9. Does methodological rigor serve inquiry, or become ritual?
10. How should methods be combined when no single method is sufficient?

# VII. Questions About Measurement, Validity, and Statistical Reasoning

## A. Measurement

1. What is being measured?
2. What construct or attribute is the measure intended to capture?
3. How is the construct operationalized?
4. Is the measure reliable?
5. Is the measure valid for this purpose?
6. Does the measurement procedure change what is being measured?
7. What sources of measurement error are likely?
8. Are there calibration standards or reference points?
9. Does the measure travel across contexts, groups, or time periods?
10. What is lost when the phenomenon is quantified?

## B. Validity

1. Is the study internally valid?
2. Is it externally valid?
3. Is it construct-valid?
4. Is it statistically valid?
5. Are these forms of validity in tension?
6. Does high internal validity come at the cost of realism or generalizability?
7. Does high ecological realism come at the cost of control?
8. Are validity claims proportional to the design?
9. Which validity threat is most serious here?
10. What would strengthen the weakest form of validity?

## C. Statistical Reasoning

1. What statistical claim is being made?
2. What is the unit of analysis?
3. What population is being generalized to?
4. What uncertainty is reported?
5. Are p-values, confidence intervals, effect sizes, or Bayesian quantities interpreted correctly?
6. Is statistical significance confused with practical importance?
7. Is an effect size reported in a meaningful way?
8. Are multiple comparisons, p-hacking, or selective reporting concerns present?
9. Are assumptions checked or merely assumed?
10. Does the statistical analysis answer the scientific question, or only a narrower technical question?

## D. Sensitivity and Robustness

1. Which assumptions matter most to the conclusion?
2. What happens if those assumptions are changed?
3. Are results robust across model specifications?
4. Are results robust across samples, measures, or analytic choices?
5. Is robustness being demonstrated or merely asserted?
6. Are sensitivity analyses designed before or after seeing results?
7. What would make the result fragile?
8. What would make the result more credible?
9. Is robustness being confused with replication?
10. How much uncertainty remains after robustness checks?

# VIII. Questions About Reproducibility, Replication, and Scientific Rigor

## A. Reproducibility

1. Could another researcher reproduce the analysis using the same data and code?
2. Are data, code, materials, and procedures available?
3. Are all exclusions, transformations, and analytic decisions documented?
4. Is the workflow transparent enough to audit?
5. Are computational environments, software versions, or preprocessing steps reported?
6. What barriers prevent reproducibility?
7. Is non-reproducibility due to error, ambiguity, proprietary data, or missing documentation?
8. How does reproducibility support scientific trust?
9. Can a result be reproducible but still false or misleading?
10. What would a reproducibility checklist reveal about this study?

## B. Replication

1. Has the result been replicated with new data?
2. Is the replication direct, conceptual, or constructive?
3. What features of the original study must be preserved for a fair replication?
4. What features should vary to test generality?
5. Is the replication adequately powered?
6. Are failed replications treated as informative or dismissed too quickly?
7. What does replication test: the result, the method, the theory, or the boundary conditions?
8. How should original and replication results be synthesized?
9. What would count as a convincing replication?
10. What would count as a productive failure to replicate?

## C. Replication Crisis and Institutional Error

1. What does the replication crisis reveal about individual reasoning?
2. What does it reveal about incentives and institutions?
3. How do publication bias and reporting bias distort the scientific record?
4. How do small samples, flexible analyses, and selective reporting create false confidence?
5. What role do journals, peer reviewers, funders, and universities play?
6. How do preregistration and registered reports change incentives?
7. How do open data and open code change accountability?
8. Are reforms aimed at methods, incentives, culture, or all three?
9. How can a field become more self-correcting?
10. What would responsible trust in a crisis-affected field look like?

## D. Rigorous Science in Practice

1. Were hypotheses specified in advance?
2. Was the sample size justified?
3. Were randomization and blinding used where appropriate?
4. Were exclusions and missing data handled transparently?
5. Were outcomes and analyses reported fully?
6. Were uncertainty and limitations communicated honestly?
7. Were alternative explanations considered?
8. Were methods documented well enough for critique?
9. Was the study designed to learn, or merely to produce a publishable result?
10. What would make this work more rigorous without making it impossible?

# IX. Questions About Science as a Social Institution

## A. Peer Review and Publishing

1. What does peer review actually check?
2. What does peer review not check?
3. How can peer review improve scientific reasoning?
4. How can peer review reinforce conservatism, prestige bias, or gatekeeping?
5. What incentives shape what gets submitted, reviewed, revised, and published?
6. How does journal prestige affect credibility judgments?
7. What are the strengths and weaknesses of anonymous, open, and post-publication review?
8. How do registered reports change the publication process?
9. When should peer review increase trust, and when should it not?
10. How should non-specialists interpret “peer-reviewed”?

## B. Publication Bias and Reporting Bias

1. What results are more likely to be published?
2. What results are less likely to be published?
3. Are null, negative, or messy findings systematically underrepresented?
4. Are outcomes selectively reported?
5. Are subgroup analyses or secondary outcomes emphasized after the fact?
6. How would publication bias affect a literature review?
7. How would reporting bias affect a meta-analysis?
8. What incentives encourage selective reporting?
9. What reforms reduce these biases?
10. How should readers adjust confidence when the literature may be biased?

## C. Metascience

1. How does metascience study science scientifically?
2. What evidence does metascience use to evaluate research practices?
3. What has metascience revealed about bias, reproducibility, and incentives?
4. How does metascience differ from philosophy of science?
5. How does metascience differ from sociology of science?
6. What reforms are supported by metascientific evidence?
7. How should fields respond to metascientific criticism?
8. Can metascience itself suffer from the same problems it studies?
9. What does a healthy scientific ecosystem look like?
10. How can metascience help science become more self-correcting?

## D. Evidence Synthesis and Systematic Review

1. What question is the review trying to answer?
2. What studies were included and excluded?
3. Was the search strategy systematic and transparent?
4. How was study quality assessed?
5. Are studies comparable enough to synthesize?
6. Is heterogeneity explored rather than ignored?
7. Are effect sizes pooled appropriately?
8. How are publication bias and reporting bias assessed?
9. Does the review distinguish evidence strength from recommendation strength?
10. What should decision-makers conclude from the synthesis?

## E. Science, Power, and Trust

1. Who funds the research?
2. Who benefits from the results?
3. Who is harmed if the result is wrong?
4. Whose questions are treated as scientifically important?
5. Whose data are collected, and whose are missing?
6. Who controls publication, access, and interpretation?
7. What conflicts of interest are present?
8. How are values embedded in research questions, models, and recommendations?
9. When is distrust of science reasonable, and when is it motivated or cynical?
10. What would trustworthy science require in this case?

# X. Questions for Reading Scientific Papers

## A. Question and Framing

1. What is the precise research question?
2. Why does the question matter?
3. Is the question framed descriptively, causally, mechanistically, or predictively?
4. What concepts are central to the question?
5. How are those concepts defined?
6. What assumptions are built into the framing?
7. What alternative framings are possible?
8. What is left outside the scope?
9. Does the paper acknowledge its scope limits?
10. Does the framing predispose the reader toward a particular conclusion?

## B. Design and Data

1. What study design is used?
2. What is the sample, population, or target system?
3. How were observations, participants, cases, or data points selected?
4. What variables are measured?
5. How reliable and valid are the measures?
6. What data are missing?
7. What transformations or exclusions were applied?
8. What comparison is central to the study?
9. Is the design appropriate for the claim?
10. What design would better answer the question?

## C. Analysis and Inference

1. What analytic method is used?
2. What assumptions does the method require?
3. Are those assumptions plausible?
4. How are uncertainty and variation represented?
5. Are effect sizes meaningful?
6. Are statistical results interpreted cautiously?
7. Are causal conclusions warranted?
8. Are alternative explanations addressed?
9. Does the analysis match the design?
10. What inference is strongest, and what inference is weakest?

## D. Theory and Explanation

1. What theory motivates the paper?
2. What model or conceptual framework is used?
3. What explanation is offered?
4. Is the explanation mechanistic, statistical, causal, or interpretive?
5. Does the evidence support the explanation or only the pattern?
6. Are theoretical virtues invoked explicitly or implicitly?
7. Is the theory revised responsibly?
8. Are ad hoc moves present?
9. What rival explanation remains live?
10. What should the next study test?

## E. Reproducibility and Institutional Context

1. Are data and code available?
2. Was the study preregistered?
3. Are materials and protocols accessible?
4. Is the journal, funder, or field known for particular incentives or biases?
5. Are conflicts of interest disclosed?
6. Has the result been replicated?
7. Is the paper part of a larger literature or an isolated finding?
8. Are systematic reviews available?
9. How would publication bias affect interpretation?
10. How much confidence should a responsible reader have?

# XI. Questions About Pseudoscience and Weak Science Clinics

## A. Diagnosing Science-y Claims

1. What scientific language is being used?
2. Is the language connected to real methods or merely decorative?
3. Are graphs, statistics, or technical terms used to create authority?
4. Are causal claims supported by appropriate evidence?
5. Are mechanisms plausible or speculative?
6. Are anecdotes treated as if they were controlled evidence?
7. Are testimonials replacing measurement?
8. Are critics represented fairly?
9. Is uncertainty acknowledged?
10. What would a fair test of the claim look like?

## B. Demarcation Case Questions

1. Does the practice generate testable predictions?
2. Does it expose itself to possible failure?
3. Does it update in response to negative evidence?
4. Does it use methods appropriate to its claims?
5. Does it rely on isolated success stories?
6. Does it explain away failures with vague or unfalsifiable claims?
7. Does it borrow credibility from legitimate science?
8. Does it cultivate distrust of mainstream criticism without evidence?
9. Does it improve over time through error correction?
10. Should the practice be classified as science, weak science, protoscience, pseudoscience, or non-scientific belief?

## C. Public Communication Questions

1. How should this claim be explained to a non-specialist?
2. What should be criticized without exaggeration?
3. What uncertainty should be preserved?
4. What is the strongest version of the claim?
5. What is the weakest link in the evidence?
6. What would a responsible headline say?
7. What would an irresponsible headline say?
8. How can critique avoid condescension while remaining clear?
9. What practical decisions might people make based on this claim?
10. What harms could follow from overbelief or underbelief?

4. Are alternatives considered?
5. Is confidence communicated clearly?

## E. Systems Thinking Connections

1. What system is being studied?
2. What feedback loops, delays, or unintended consequences matter?
3. What institutional system produced the research?
4. How might the research change the system it studies?
5. What leverage points for better science are visible?

## F. Model Thinking Connections

1. What model is explicit or implicit?
2. What is the model’s purpose?
3. What assumptions structure the model?
4. How is the model calibrated or validated?
5. What are the model’s limits and ethical implications?
