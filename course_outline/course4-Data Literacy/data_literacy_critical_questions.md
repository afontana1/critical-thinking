# Critical Questions for Course 4: Data Literacy – From Measurement to Data Ecosystems

## Purpose of This Question Bank

These questions are designed to support discussion, writing, argument mapping, case analysis, data critique, capstone projects, and metacognitive reflection for the course **Data Literacy – From Measurement to Data Ecosystems**.

The central aim is to help students ask not merely, “What do the numbers say?” but:

- Where did these numbers come from?
- What was measured, how, and by whom?
- What population or reality do these data supposedly represent?
- What uncertainty, bias, or institutional filtering shaped the dataset before analysis began?
- What conclusions are justified, and what conclusions exceed the data?
- How do data infrastructures, platforms, research practices, and institutions shape what becomes visible, measurable, credible, and actionable?

4. Explain how a graph can be true but misleading.
5. Explain the difference between statistical significance and practical importance.

8. Explain why missing data can be evidence of a hidden selection process.

10. Explain how quantification can both increase and undermine trust.

## C. Exam or Essay Questions

1. Reconstruct a quantitative public claim as an argument from data, then evaluate its sampling, measurement, and inferential weaknesses.

3. Analyze how measurement decisions affect the ethical and epistemic status of a dataset.
4. Explain why statistical inference should be understood as principled argument rather than mechanical calculation.

7. Analyze how research reporting guidelines improve evidential trust while remaining insufficient by themselves.
8. Discuss how big-data infrastructures transform the epistemology and politics of measurement.
9. Evaluate a data ecosystem in terms of provenance, governance, bias, interoperability, and accountability.
10. Defend a model of responsible data literacy that includes technical, epistemic, and ethical virtues.
