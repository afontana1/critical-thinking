# Critical Questions for Course 5: Causality and Causal Reasoning

## Purpose of This Question Bank

These questions are designed to help students reason critically about causal claims across philosophy, science, epidemiology, economics, law, public policy, and AI. They are meant for class discussion, reading responses, exams, writing assignments, causal diagrams, case analyses, and capstone projects.

The central habit this course should cultivate is: **do not merely ask whether two things are associated; ask what causal question is being asked, what comparison is required, what assumptions are being made, what design supports the inference, what mechanisms could explain it, and how far the claim can responsibly travel.**

---

# I. Foundations: What Is a Causal Claim?

## A. Identifying Causal Claims

1. What makes a claim causal rather than merely descriptive, predictive, or correlational?
2. What causal verb or causal implication is being used: causes, increases, reduces, prevents, leads to, contributes to, predicts, explains, or is associated with?
3. Is the speaker explicitly claiming causation, or merely allowing the audience to infer it?
4. What would have to be different in the world for this causal claim to be false?
5. What is the implied effect or outcome, and has it been clearly specified?
6. What is the implied cause, exposure, treatment, intervention, event, or condition?
7. Is the causal claim about an individual case, a population pattern, a general relation, or a policy intervention?
8. Does the claim confuse “X came before Y” with “X caused Y”?
9. Does the claim treat a risk factor as if it were a sufficient cause?
10. Does the claim treat a predictor as if it were an intervention point?
11. Is the causal claim being used to explain, blame, justify policy, sell something, or guide action?
12. How would the claim change if rewritten in strictly correlational language?
13. How would the claim change if rewritten as a precise causal question?
14. What uncertainty is hidden by the grammar of the sentence?
15. What alternative causal stories are being ruled out, and on what basis?

## B. Difference-Making and Counterfactual Thinking

1. What difference is the alleged cause supposed to make?
2. Compared to what alternative condition?
3. What is the relevant counterfactual: what would have happened if the cause had been absent, altered, delayed, intensified, or replaced?
4. Is the comparison between two real groups, one real group and an imagined alternative, or two hypothetical scenarios?
5. Are the counterfactual alternatives plausible, relevant, and close enough to the actual case?
6. What background conditions are being held fixed in the counterfactual comparison?
7. Would the effect have occurred anyway without the alleged cause?
8. Would the effect have occurred at the same time, in the same way, or to the same degree?
9. Does the claim require a but-for cause, a contributory cause, or a sufficient causal pathway?
10. What makes the chosen counterfactual more appropriate than other possible counterfactuals?
11. Does the counterfactual depend on unrealistic assumptions about human behavior, institutions, or physical systems?
12. Are there multiple possible counterfactual baselines, and do they support different conclusions?

## C. Kinds of Causes

1. Is the alleged cause necessary, sufficient, contributory, enabling, triggering, or background?
2. Is the cause a single event, an ongoing condition, a structural factor, a mechanism, or a policy intervention?
3. Is the claim about general causation or specific causation?
4. Is the cause supposed to be direct, indirect, proximate, remote, or mediated through other factors?
5. Are multiple causes interacting rather than acting independently?
6. Is the effect overdetermined by several sufficient causes?
7. Is the causal claim about increasing probability rather than guaranteeing an outcome?
8. Does the argument mistake a necessary condition for a sufficient condition?
9. Does it mistake a background condition for the active cause?
10. Does it identify a trigger while ignoring the system that made the trigger effective?
11. Is the causal relation stable, context-dependent, or highly contingent?
12. What role do timing, dose, sequence, and duration play in the alleged causal relation?

## D. Causal Language in Public Discourse

1. What causal claims are hidden in headlines, slogans, dashboards, advertisements, or policy briefs?
2. Is causal language being softened to avoid responsibility or strengthened to persuade?
3. Does the claim move from “associated with” to “caused by” without justification?
4. Does it use vague causal language such as “linked to,” “tied to,” “connected with,” or “drives”?
5. What would a more honest version of the causal claim say?
6. Does the public-facing claim preserve the limits of the underlying evidence?
7. Does it exaggerate certainty, magnitude, generality, or policy relevance?
8. Who benefits if the causal claim is accepted as stated?
9. Who bears the cost if the causal claim is wrong?
10. What causal uncertainty is being compressed into a simple narrative?

# II. Theories and Frameworks of Causation

## A. Counterfactual Accounts

1. What counterfactual dependence is being asserted between cause and effect?
2. Would the effect have occurred if the cause had not occurred?
3. Does the case involve overdetermination, preemption, backup causes, or causal chains?
4. Does the counterfactual account explain why the cause matters for intervention?
5. How are “nearby” possible worlds or relevant alternatives being selected?
6. Could different choices of relevant alternatives produce different causal judgments?
7. Does the counterfactual analysis clarify the claim or make it more ambiguous?
8. Does the causal claim depend on a counterfactual that cannot be responsibly evaluated?
9. What evidence could help support or weaken the counterfactual comparison?
10. Is the counterfactual question being confused with a moral or legal responsibility question?

## B. Interventionist Accounts

1. What intervention on X would test whether X affects Y?
2. Is the intervention well-defined, or is “changing X” ambiguous?
3. Would intervening on X leave other relevant factors unchanged?
4. Is X manipulable in practice, or only conceptually?
5. Does the causal claim matter because it identifies something we can change?
6. Would an intervention on X produce the same effect in different contexts?
7. Does the proposed intervention alter the system in ways that differ from naturally occurring variation in X?
8. Are we asking what causes Y or what intervention would change Y?
9. Does the interventionist framing ignore mechanisms that matter for implementation?
10. Is the proposed causal variable too broad, vague, or bundled to support meaningful intervention?

## C. Mechanistic Accounts

1. What mechanism is supposed to connect X to Y?
2. What are the intermediate steps in the causal chain?
3. Which step in the mechanism is best supported by evidence?
4. Which step is weakest, speculative, or merely assumed?
5. Does the mechanism explain timing, dosage, scope, and heterogeneity of effects?
6. Could the same observed association arise through a different mechanism?
7. Does mechanism evidence support causation even when statistical evidence is incomplete?
8. Does statistical evidence support causation even when mechanism evidence is missing?
9. What would we expect to observe if the proposed mechanism were operating?
10. What would count as evidence that the mechanism is absent, blocked, or overridden?
11. Does the mechanism depend on background conditions that may not hold elsewhere?
12. Is the mechanism biological, psychological, institutional, technological, economic, or social?

## D. Capacities and Context

1. Does the alleged cause have a stable capacity, or does it work only under special arrangements?
2. What background conditions allow the causal capacity to manifest?
3. What prevents the capacity from producing the effect in some contexts?
4. Is the causal claim being overgeneralized from one setting to another?
5. Does the argument assume that a cause that worked “there” will work “here”?
6. What contextual features are essential to the effect?
7. What would need to be true for the causal relation to travel across populations, institutions, or historical periods?
8. Does the evidence show that X can cause Y, that X usually causes Y, or that X caused Y here?
9. How should policy change when causal capacities are context-sensitive?
10. Does the argument confuse internal validity with external validity?

## E. Causal Pluralism

1. Which causal framework is most appropriate for the question: counterfactual, interventionist, mechanistic, capacity-based, legal, or statistical?
2. What does each framework reveal that the others obscure?
3. Are different disciplines using the word “cause” differently?
4. Does the dispute arise from evidence, values, definitions, or different causal concepts?
5. Can multiple causal frameworks support the same conclusion?
6. Do the frameworks point in different directions?
7. When should we accept plural causal evidence rather than demand one decisive method?
8. Does the causal question require explanation, prediction, intervention, attribution, or responsibility?
9. What would be lost by forcing all causal reasoning into one model?
10. How can pluralism avoid becoming “anything goes”?

# III. Causal Designs, Identification, and Validity

## A. From Causal Question to Design

1. What exact causal question is being asked?
2. What treatment, exposure, intervention, or event is being compared to what alternative?
3. What is the target population?
4. What is the outcome, and how is it measured?
5. What time horizon matters for the causal effect?
6. What design would best approximate the relevant counterfactual?
7. What design was actually used?
8. Does the design match the causal question?
9. What comparison group is being used, and why is it credible?
10. What assumptions must hold for the comparison to identify a causal effect?
11. Are the assumptions stated, hidden, or ignored?
12. What would make the design fail?
13. Is the design strong enough for the action being recommended?
14. Is the study estimating the effect of assignment, treatment received, exposure, compliance, or implementation?
15. What is the unit of analysis, and does it match the causal process?

## B. Experiments and Randomization

1. What does randomization accomplish in this design?
2. What does randomization not accomplish?
3. Were participants, units, or clusters randomized?
4. Was assignment concealed or vulnerable to manipulation?
5. Was blinding possible, and does lack of blinding threaten the result?
6. Did randomization balance relevant background factors in practice?
7. Were there spillovers between treatment and control groups?
8. Did noncompliance undermine the intended comparison?
9. Was attrition balanced or informative?
10. Does the experiment estimate the effect of offering treatment or receiving treatment?
11. Is the experimental setting artificial in ways that matter for generalization?
12. Is the control condition a genuine alternative or a misleading baseline?
13. Are the outcomes meaningful or merely convenient to measure?
14. Are subgroup effects exploratory or pre-specified?
15. Does the experiment answer the policy question people are using it to answer?

## C. Quasi-Experiments and Natural Experiments

1. What source of variation is being treated as “as good as random”?
2. Why should we believe that source of variation is exogenous?
3. What institutional rule, shock, threshold, lottery, or timing event creates the comparison?
4. Could units manipulate their exposure to the treatment?
5. Are treated and comparison groups comparable before treatment?
6. What rival explanation could mimic the estimated effect?
7. What falsification or placebo test would strengthen credibility?
8. What would count as evidence against the identifying assumption?
9. Is the “natural experiment” label doing more rhetorical work than methodological work?
10. Is the effect local to a narrow group, time, or institutional setting?
11. What assumptions are needed to move from the quasi-experimental estimate to a broader causal claim?
12. Does the design identify the effect of a policy, a signal, a threshold, an incentive, or a bundle of changes?

## D. Identification Versus Estimation

1. Is the design capable of identifying a causal effect at all?
2. Is the estimate precise but causally unconvincing?
3. Is the design credible but the estimate imprecise?
4. What assumptions identify the effect?
5. What statistical model estimates the effect?
6. Are identification and estimation being conflated?
7. Does the analysis rely on model complexity to compensate for weak design?
8. Would a simpler design with fewer data be more credible than a sophisticated model with biased data?
9. What is the estimand: average treatment effect, local average treatment effect, effect on the treated, direct effect, total effect, or something else?
10. Does the reported estimate correspond to the causal quantity people care about?
11. What is the difference between “we can estimate a number” and “we have identified a causal effect” in this case?

## E. Internal Validity

1. What is the main threat to internal validity?
2. Could confounding explain the observed association?
3. Could reverse causation explain the result?
4. Could selection into treatment explain the result?
5. Could differential attrition explain the result?
6. Could measurement error distort the estimated effect?
7. Could spillovers or interference violate the comparison?
8. Could regression to the mean create the appearance of improvement?
9. Could history, maturation, or secular trends explain the change?
10. Could the outcome have changed for reasons unrelated to the intervention?
11. Are there unmeasured variables that plausibly affect both treatment and outcome?
12. Does the study include robustness checks that target the most serious threat?
13. Are threats to validity acknowledged as central or treated as afterthoughts?
14. Which single threat, if true, would most seriously undermine the causal claim?

## F. External Validity and Transportability

1. To whom, where, and when is the causal claim supposed to generalize?
2. Is the study population similar to the target population?
3. Are the institutional conditions similar enough for the effect to travel?
4. Does the mechanism depend on local context?
5. Are there reasons to expect heterogeneous effects across subgroups?
6. Are implementation conditions likely to differ outside the study setting?
7. Does scaling up change the causal process?
8. Does the intervention interact with culture, incentives, law, infrastructure, or history?
9. What would need to be replicated before using the result elsewhere?
10. Does the argument distinguish “worked in this study” from “will work as policy”?
11. What mechanism evidence supports transportability?
12. What contextual evidence warns against transportability?

# IV. Causal Diagrams and the Logic of Adjustment

## A. Drawing Causal Diagrams

1. What variables belong in the causal diagram?
2. What are the plausible arrows among them?
3. Which arrows are supported by theory, evidence, or background knowledge?
4. Which arrows are speculative but important?
5. What variables are missing from the diagram?
6. Does the diagram represent a causal story or merely a statistical association?
7. Are time order and causal order clearly represented?
8. Does the diagram make hidden assumptions visible?
9. Could a rival diagram explain the same observed association?
10. What would distinguish the rival diagrams empirically?
11. Does the diagram oversimplify a feedback system into an acyclic structure?
12. Is the diagram being used as a thinking tool or as false proof?

## B. Confounders

1. What variables might cause both X and Y?
2. Are exposed and unexposed groups comparable on relevant background factors?
3. Which backdoor paths need to be blocked?
4. What evidence supports treating a variable as a confounder?
5. Are all important confounders measured?
6. Are some confounders measured poorly?
7. Does adjustment for confounders make the groups more comparable?
8. Could residual confounding remain after adjustment?
9. Is the claim vulnerable to unmeasured confounding?
10. What sensitivity analysis would show how strong unmeasured confounding would need to be?

## C. Mediators

1. Is this variable part of the causal pathway from X to Y?
2. Are we interested in the total effect or the direct effect?
3. Would controlling for this mediator remove part of the effect we want to estimate?
4. Does the mediator help explain how the effect occurs?
5. Is the mediator measured after treatment or exposure?
6. Does adjustment for the mediator answer a different causal question?
7. Are there multiple mediating pathways?
8. Could the mediator also be confounded with the outcome?
9. Does the argument treat mechanism variables as if they were mere controls?
10. What would a mechanism map add beyond a DAG?

## D. Colliders

1. Is this variable a common effect of two or more causes?
2. Are we conditioning on a collider by selecting, stratifying, adjusting, or restricting the sample?
3. Could conditioning on this variable create a spurious association?
4. Is the dataset itself selected through a collider process?
5. Are we only observing people who entered treatment, were hospitalized, applied, survived, responded, or were detected?
6. Could Berkson-type selection distort the result?
7. Does the analysis control for a variable because it is available rather than because it should be controlled?
8. What association might appear only after conditioning on the collider?
9. How would the conclusion change if collider bias were removed?
10. What warning signs indicate that “more controls” may be harmful here?

## E. Simpson’s Paradox and Stratification

1. Does the association change direction after stratifying by a third variable?
2. Is the third variable a confounder, mediator, collider, or effect modifier?
3. Which level of aggregation answers the causal question?
4. Does the aggregate association hide subgroup differences?
5. Does the subgroup association hide population-level structure?
6. Is Simpson’s paradox being treated as a statistical trick rather than a causal warning?
7. What causal story explains the reversal?
8. Should the analysis report pooled results, stratified results, or both?
9. What decision would be different if we looked only at aggregate data?
10. How does this case show that “the data speak for themselves” is misleading?

# V. Epidemiology and Public Health

## A. Moving from Association to Causation

1. What association has been observed?
2. What causal interpretation is being considered?
3. Does the exposure precede the outcome?
4. What confounders are most plausible?
5. What forms of bias could explain the association?
6. Is there a dose-response pattern?
7. Is the association consistent across studies, populations, and methods?
8. Is there a plausible mechanism?
9. Is the effect coherent with existing biological, social, or clinical knowledge?
10. Does the evidence support causation, or merely justify further investigation?
11. Are Bradford Hill considerations being used thoughtfully or mechanically?
12. Which consideration matters most in this case, and why?

## B. Risk, Magnitude, and Communication

1. Is the claim stated in absolute risk, relative risk, odds, hazard, or vague language?
2. Does the relative risk sound dramatic while the absolute risk is small?
3. What is the baseline risk?
4. What is the risk difference?
5. What is the number needed to treat or harm, if relevant?
6. Does the communication distinguish individual risk from population impact?
7. Are uncertainty intervals reported and explained?
8. Are subgroup differences important?
9. Does the message support informed action or manipulate fear?
10. What would a numerically honest public-health statement look like?

## C. General and Specific Causation

1. Does the evidence show that X can cause Y in general?
2. Does the evidence show that X caused Y in this particular case?
3. What additional evidence is needed for specific causation?
4. Does population-level risk evidence justify individual attribution?
5. Are there alternative causes in the individual case?
6. Is the timing compatible with the alleged causal process?
7. Does mechanism evidence connect the exposure to the specific outcome?
8. What standard of proof is being applied?
9. Is the individual case being treated as if it were a population study?
10. Is the population study being overextended to settle an individual case?

## D. Public Health Under Uncertainty

1. How much causal evidence is enough to act?
2. What are the costs of acting too soon?
3. What are the costs of waiting too long?
4. Does the precautionary principle apply?
5. Are decision thresholds being confused with proof thresholds?
6. What evidence would change the recommended public-health action?
7. Are uncertainty and disagreement being communicated responsibly?
8. Does the policy depend on uptake, compliance, trust, or institutional capacity?
9. Could an intervention that works biologically fail socially?
10. Does the causal claim support the actual policy being proposed?

# VI. Economics and Social-Scientific Causal Inference

## A. Natural Experiments and Credible Variation

1. What variation is being used to identify the causal effect?
2. Why is that variation plausibly exogenous?
3. What institutional detail makes the design credible?
4. What would undermine the claim that the variation is as-if random?
5. Are people or institutions able to sort around the variation?
6. Does the design identify a local or general effect?
7. What comparison is being made, and why is it meaningful?
8. What pre-treatment evidence supports comparability?
9. What alternative explanations remain plausible?
10. Is the study persuasive because of design or because of statistical sophistication?

## B. Instrumental Variables

1. What is the instrument?
2. Why should the instrument affect the treatment?
3. Why should the instrument affect the outcome only through the treatment?
4. Is the exclusion restriction plausible?
5. Could the instrument be correlated with unmeasured confounders?
6. Who are the compliers, and whose effect is being estimated?
7. Is the local average treatment effect relevant to the policy question?
8. Is the instrument strong enough to be informative?
9. What story makes the instrument credible?
10. What story would make the instrument invalid?

## C. Difference-in-Differences

1. What treated and comparison groups are being compared?
2. What are the pre-treatment trends?
3. Is the parallel trends assumption plausible?
4. What could cause the groups to trend differently even without treatment?
5. Are there other simultaneous policy changes?
6. Are there anticipation effects?
7. Are there spillovers between groups?
8. Are placebo outcomes or placebo time periods tested?
9. Is the effect immediate, delayed, temporary, or cumulative?
10. Does the estimate depend heavily on modeling choices?

## D. Regression Discontinuity

1. What threshold assigns treatment?
2. Are units just above and just below the cutoff comparable?
3. Could units manipulate their position around the cutoff?
4. Is there bunching near the threshold?
5. Is the effect local to the cutoff?
6. Does the local effect generalize beyond the cutoff population?
7. Are outcomes smooth around the cutoff except for the treatment?
8. Are covariates balanced near the threshold?
9. Is the bandwidth choice justified?
10. What does the RD design answer, and what does it not answer?

## E. Credibility Checks

1. What falsification test would most directly challenge the causal interpretation?
2. What placebo outcome should not be affected if the theory is right?
3. What placebo treatment should not produce an effect?
4. Are results robust to reasonable alternative specifications?
5. Are robustness checks targeted or merely decorative?
6. What sensitivity analysis is needed?
7. What result would the authors have found embarrassing?
8. Is disconfirming evidence reported or ignored?
9. Does the study invite replication with other data or designs?
10. How does the credibility of the design compare with the size of the claim?

# VII. Law, Responsibility, and Causation

## A. Legal Causation as Normative Causal Judgment

1. Why does law need causal concepts that differ from scientific concepts?
2. What institutional purpose does the causal judgment serve?
3. Is the court asking what happened, who is responsible, or how far liability should extend?
4. Does the legal question require factual causation, proximate causation, or both?
5. How do fairness, foreseeability, policy, and responsibility shape the causal judgment?
6. Is “cause” being used descriptively or normatively?
7. What would scientific evidence settle, and what would remain a legal judgment?
8. Does the legal conclusion depend on a standard of proof rather than certainty?
9. How does law manage causal uncertainty when a decision cannot be postponed?
10. When is legal causation narrower or broader than scientific causation?

## B. Factual Cause and But-For Reasoning

1. But for the defendant’s action, would the harm have occurred?
2. Is the but-for test appropriate in this case?
3. Does the case involve overdetermination or multiple sufficient causes?
4. Does the but-for test fail because several causes could each have produced the harm?
5. Is material contribution a better framework?
6. What causal chain links action to harm?
7. Are there intervening causes?
8. Are background conditions being mistaken for responsible causes?
9. Is the harm defined too broadly or too narrowly?
10. What evidence supports the factual-causation claim?

## C. Proximate Cause and Scope of Liability

1. Was the harm foreseeable?
2. Was the harm within the scope of the risk created by the defendant?
3. Is the causal chain too remote for liability?
4. Did an intervening act break the chain of responsibility?
5. Is the proximate-cause judgment really a policy judgment?
6. Does the legal argument distinguish causal contribution from legal responsibility?
7. What would happen if liability extended to every factual consequence?
8. What normative boundary is the court drawing?
9. Would different legal doctrines draw the boundary differently?
10. What does this case show about the limits of purely scientific causation?

## D. Scientific Evidence in Court

1. What causal question is the expert evidence supposed to answer?
2. Is the evidence about general causation or specific causation?
3. Are epidemiological studies being interpreted appropriately?
4. Does the expert explain mechanism, dose, timing, and alternative causes?
5. Are probabilistic claims being translated responsibly into legal claims?
6. What standard of admissibility applies?
7. Are Daubert-style reliability factors satisfied?
8. Is the expert’s method reliable in general and properly applied here?
9. Does the court understand the limits of the scientific evidence?
10. Is legal proof being confused with scientific certainty?

# VIII. AI, Machine Learning, and Prediction

## A. Prediction Versus Causation

1. Does the model predict an outcome, explain an outcome, or identify an intervention?
2. Is the predictor a cause, a proxy, a symptom, or a correlate?
3. Would changing the predictor change the outcome?
4. Could the model perform well while misunderstanding the causal structure?
5. Is the model useful for prediction but dangerous for policy?
6. Does predictive accuracy hide confounding or selection bias?
7. Are decision-makers treating model features as causal levers?
8. What causal question remains unanswered by the predictive model?
9. What intervention would be justified by this model, if any?
10. Does the model generalize when the environment changes?

## B. Causal Discovery

1. Is causal discovery being used to generate hypotheses or to settle conclusions?
2. What assumptions does the discovery method require?
3. Are those assumptions plausible in this domain?
4. Could hidden confounders distort the discovered structure?
5. Could feedback loops violate the model assumptions?
6. Does domain knowledge support the proposed causal graph?
7. What experimental, quasi-experimental, or mechanism evidence would be needed next?
8. Are causal-discovery outputs being overinterpreted because they look technical?
9. What rival causal structures remain possible?
10. How should uncertainty about the causal graph be communicated?

## C. Counterfactual Fairness and Algorithmic Ethics

1. What counterfactual change is being imagined in the fairness claim?
2. Which protected attribute or pathway is being considered causally relevant?
3. Which downstream effects of protected attributes should count as unfair?
4. Does the fairness criterion depend on controversial causal assumptions?
5. Are proxy variables carrying the influence of protected attributes?
6. Would the decision have changed in a relevant counterfactual world?
7. What causal model of society is built into the fairness analysis?
8. Does the analysis distinguish discrimination, structural inequality, and legitimate risk factors?
9. Can the fairness goal be achieved by removing variables, or does the causal structure remain?
10. How should uncertainty about fairness-relevant causal pathways affect deployment?

## D. Feedback Loops and Adaptive Systems

1. Does the model’s prediction change the behavior it predicts?
2. Does the system learn from data that its own decisions helped create?
3. Could feedback loops amplify inequality, bias, or error?
4. Does the causal structure change over time because of the model’s deployment?
5. Are there delayed or indirect harms?
6. Does the model create incentives that alter the target population?
7. Are monitoring and updating procedures in place?
8. Does a static DAG capture the dynamics, or is systems thinking needed?
9. What intervention would reduce harmful feedback?
10. How would we know if the deployed model caused harm?

# IX. Mechanisms, Cases, and Qualitative Causal Reasoning

## A. Mechanism Mapping

1. What is the proposed causal chain from X to Y?
2. What are the intermediate steps?
3. Which links are supported by evidence?
4. Which links are assumed?
5. Where is the mechanism most likely to fail?
6. Are there alternative mechanisms producing the same outcome?
7. Does the mechanism explain who is affected and who is not?
8. Does it explain timing, sequence, and dose-response?
9. Does it support transfer to new contexts?
10. What evidence would strengthen the weakest link?

## B. Process Tracing

1. What rival causal stories are being compared within the case?
2. What evidence would be expected if each story were true?
3. What evidence would be surprising if each story were false?
4. Is a piece of evidence a hoop test, smoking-gun test, straw-in-the-wind test, or doubly decisive test?
5. Does the evidence eliminate rivals or merely support one story?
6. Are sequence and timing crucial?
7. Are documents, testimony, traces, and behavior being interpreted in context?
8. Could the same case evidence fit multiple causal narratives?
9. What missing evidence would matter most?
10. Does the case analysis avoid becoming mere storytelling?

## C. Case Studies and Theory Development

1. Is the case being used to generate, refine, or test a causal theory?
2. Why was this case selected?
3. Is it typical, deviant, crucial, extreme, or illustrative?
4. What can this case show that a large-N study might miss?
5. What can this case not show?
6. Are concepts and causal categories being clarified through the case?
7. Does the case reveal mechanisms that travel elsewhere?
8. Does the case depend on unique historical or institutional conditions?
9. What comparison case would strengthen the analysis?
10. How can case evidence be systematic rather than anecdotal?

## D. QCA, Conjunctural Causation, and Equifinality

1. Does the outcome arise from combinations of conditions rather than a single cause?
2. Are there multiple causal pathways to the same outcome?
3. Is a condition necessary, sufficient, both, or neither?
4. Does the analysis allow for causal asymmetry?
5. Could the causes of success differ from the causes of failure?
6. Are cases coded responsibly?
7. Are thresholds and set memberships justified?
8. Does the causal recipe make substantive sense?
9. Does QCA reveal complexity that regression-style thinking might flatten?
10. Does QCA overstate causal clarity from limited cases?

## E. Triangulation and Consilience

1. What different kinds of evidence support the causal claim?
2. Are the evidential sources genuinely independent?
3. Do different methods have different biases?
4. Do the findings converge despite different weaknesses?
5. Could the apparent convergence arise from shared assumptions or common data sources?
6. What evidence would provide the strongest independent check?
7. Does mechanism evidence align with design-based evidence?
8. Does case evidence align with statistical evidence?
9. Are conflicting strands of evidence acknowledged?
10. At what point does a rope of imperfect evidence become strong enough for action?

# X. Judging and Communicating Causal Claims

## A. Effect Size, Significance, and Heterogeneity

1. How large is the estimated effect?
2. Is the effect practically important or merely statistically detectable?
3. Is the effect reported in meaningful units?
4. Are absolute and relative effects distinguished?
5. Is there heterogeneity across people, places, institutions, or time?
6. Does the average effect hide important subgroup differences?
7. Are subgroup analyses credible or exploratory?
8. Does the effect matter for the decision at hand?
9. How does the effect compare with costs, risks, and alternatives?
10. Could a small individual effect have a large population impact?

## B. Uncertainty and Robustness

1. What are the main sources of uncertainty?
2. Is uncertainty statistical, causal, measurement-based, model-based, or contextual?
3. Are intervals explained as ranges of reasonable values?
4. Are robustness checks reported and substantively interpreted?
5. How sensitive is the conclusion to assumptions?
6. What assumptions matter most?
7. What would overturn the conclusion?
8. Are null results interpreted responsibly?
9. Does the communication distinguish uncertainty from ignorance?
10. Is uncertainty being used to avoid action or to justify overconfidence?

## C. Common Causal Mistakes

1. Is the argument relying on post hoc reasoning?
2. Is it vulnerable to reverse causation?
3. Does it ignore selection bias?
4. Does it mistake regression to the mean for treatment success?
5. Does it neglect base rates?
6. Does it overcontrol by adjusting for mediators or colliders?
7. Does it undercontrol by ignoring confounders?
8. Does it confuse prediction with intervention?
9. Does it treat a mechanism story as proof without evidence?
10. Does it treat an effect estimate as self-interpreting?
11. Does it overgeneralize from one context?
12. Does it use causal language that is stronger than the design permits?
13. Does it cherry-pick favorable studies or specifications?
14. Does it rely on garden-of-forking-paths analysis?
15. Does it hide uncertainty behind technical language?

## D. Writing Honest Causal Summaries

1. What is the causal question?
2. What is the comparison or counterfactual?
3. What design supports the claim?
4. What assumptions are required?
5. What is the estimated effect?
6. How uncertain is the estimate?
7. What threats to validity remain?
8. What mechanisms are plausible?
9. What evidence supports or weakens those mechanisms?
10. What populations or contexts does the claim apply to?
11. What should not be inferred from the evidence?
12. What would change our confidence?
13. What decision, if any, does the evidence support?
14. How can the claim be stated without exaggeration?
15. How can uncertainty be communicated without making the result useless?

## E. Using Causal Claims in Decisions

1. Is the evidence good enough for this decision?
2. What are the costs of false positive and false negative causal judgments?
3. What are the consequences of acting, not acting, or waiting?
4. Is more evidence feasible before action is required?
5. Are the stakes symmetrical or asymmetrical?
6. Does the decision require certainty, preponderance, precaution, or best available evidence?
7. Who benefits from the decision?
8. Who bears the risk of causal error?
9. Are values being hidden inside causal language?
10. Does the causal evidence support the policy, legal judgment, medical recommendation, or personal choice being proposed?

## B. The “Do Not Overclaim” Checklist

1. Do not say “causes” if the evidence only shows association.
2. Do not say “proves” if the evidence supports probabilistic judgment.
3. Do not generalize beyond the study population without argument.
4. Do not treat prediction as intervention.
5. Do not treat statistical significance as practical importance.
6. Do not ignore absolute risk.
7. Do not adjust for variables merely because they are available.
8. Do not hide assumptions behind technical methods.
9. Do not use mechanism stories as substitutes for evidence.
10. Do not use uncertainty selectively to defend preferred conclusions.
