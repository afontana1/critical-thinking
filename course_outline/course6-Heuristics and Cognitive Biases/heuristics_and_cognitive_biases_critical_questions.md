# Critical Questions for Course: Heuristics and Cognitive Biases

## Purpose of this Question Bank

These questions are designed to provoke critical thinking throughout the course **Heuristics and Cognitive Biases – From Fast Judgments to Better Decision-Making**. They are not merely comprehension checks. They are meant to help students examine how judgment works under uncertainty, how shortcuts can be adaptive or misleading, how biases become social and institutional, and how better decision environments can be designed.

The questions can be used for discussion, journals, essays, exams, labs, capstone projects, decision audits, calibration exercises, red-team work, and real-world analysis.

# I. Foundations: Heuristics, Bounded Rationality, and Ecological Fit

## Heuristics as Tools, Not Just Errors

1. When is a heuristic a sign of cognitive limitation, and when is it a sign of intelligence adapted to real constraints?
2. What would it mean to evaluate a reasoning strategy by its fit to an environment rather than by comparison to an ideal calculation?
3. Can a shortcut be rational even when it sometimes produces error?

5. When might a simple rule outperform a more complex model?
6. What assumptions about the world does a particular heuristic rely on?
7. How can we tell whether a heuristic failed because the heuristic is bad or because the environment changed?
8. What is the difference between being fast and being careless?
9. What is the difference between being precise and being wise?
10. Are humans biased because they use shortcuts, or because they use shortcuts in the wrong environments?

## Bounded Rationality

1. What limits does bounded rationality place on real decision-making?
2. How should reasoning standards change when agents have limited time, incomplete information, and high stakes?
3. Is “optimal” reasoning always the right standard for human judgment?
4. How do limited attention and memory shape what counts as a reasonable decision process?
5. When does demanding more analysis become irrational?

8. What kinds of problems are poorly suited to intuition alone?

## Ecological Rationality

1. What features of an environment determine whether a heuristic works well?
2. What information is naturally available in the environment, and what information must be constructed or inferred?
3. Does this environment reward speed, accuracy, caution, creativity, conformity, or independence?
