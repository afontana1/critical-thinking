# Critical Questions for Analytical Thinking

## Course Alignment

These questions are designed for the course **Analytical Thinking – From Problem Framing to Judgment Under Uncertainty**. They are meant to provoke disciplined analysis of messy problems: framing questions clearly, structuring complexity, separating evidence from inference, comparing alternatives, judging uncertainty, and producing responsible recommendations.

The questions can be used for class discussion, journals, analytic briefs, capstones, peer review, red-team exercises, data critiques, policy memos, root-cause analysis labs, and forecasting portfolios.

---

# 1. Foundations of Analytical Thinking

## 1.1 What Counts as Analysis?

1. What makes a piece of thinking genuinely analytical rather than merely opinionated, intuitive, descriptive, or reactive?
2. When is intuition a useful starting point, and when does it become an obstacle to analysis?
3. What is the difference between having a strong impression and having a justified conclusion?
4. How can analysis discipline intuition without pretending to eliminate it?
5. What makes a conclusion analytically responsible even when it remains uncertain?
6. What distinguishes analytical thinking from critical thinking, creative thinking, and problem solving?
7. Can analysis be too rigid, overstructured, or detached from context? What would that look like?
8. When does analysis clarify a problem, and when does it create a false sense of clarity?
9. What habits separate a careful analyst from a clever rationalizer?
10. How can one tell whether an analysis is serving truth, decision-making, persuasion, or institutional convenience?

## 1.2 Problem Framing and Scope

1. What exactly is the problem, and who gets to define it?
2. What would change if the problem were framed differently?
3. What is included in the frame, what is excluded, and what follows from that boundary choice?
4. Is this really one problem, or several problems being bundled together?
5. Is the stated problem a symptom, a cause, a consequence, or a value conflict?
6. What practical decision or judgment is this analysis supposed to support?
7. What is the smallest version of the problem that can still be usefully analyzed?
8. What is the larger system in which this problem sits?
9. What would count as success in solving or improving this problem?
10. Is the problem being framed in a way that already favors one solution?
11. What assumptions are hidden in the wording of the problem?
12. What would someone who is harmed by the current framing notice that others might miss?
13. What would be lost if the scope were narrowed? What would be lost if it were expanded?
14. Is the problem urgent, important, both, or neither?
15. What kind of uncertainty is most central to the problem: factual, causal, ethical, operational, political, or strategic?

## 1.3 Clarifying Terms, Categories, and Assumptions

1. Which key terms need definition before analysis can proceed?
2. Are people using the same term to mean different things?
3. Which categories are being treated as natural, when they may actually be constructed or contested?
4. What assumptions are necessary for the analysis to work?
5. Which assumptions are empirical, and which are normative?
6. Which assumptions are plausible but untested?
7. Which assumptions are convenient but fragile?
8. Which assumption, if false, would most seriously damage the conclusion?
9. Are there hidden value judgments inside seemingly technical categories?
10. Are we treating a spectrum as if it were a binary?
11. Are we treating a complex system as if it were a simple object?
12. Are we treating a temporary pattern as if it were a stable property?
13. What would someone from another discipline define differently?
14. What background model of human behavior, institutions, or incentives is being assumed?
15. Are the assumptions stated clearly enough for someone else to challenge them?

## 1.4 Evidence, Inference, and Interpretation

1. What is the raw information, and what has already been interpreted?
2. What does the evidence directly show?
3. What are we inferring beyond what the evidence directly shows?
4. What broader interpretation is being placed on the inference?
5. Where does the analysis move from observation to judgment?
6. Is the same evidence compatible with multiple interpretations?
7. What evidence would distinguish among those interpretations?
8. Are we confusing correlation, association, sequence, or co-occurrence with explanation?
9. Are we presenting an inference as if it were a fact?
10. Are we presenting an interpretation as if it were the only reasonable interpretation?
11. What would a skeptical but fair reader challenge first?
12. How strong is the connection between the evidence and the conclusion?
13. What important evidence is missing?
14. What evidence would change the conclusion most dramatically?
15. What level of confidence is warranted, and why?

# 2. Problem Decomposition and Representation

## 2.1 Breaking Problems Apart

1. What are the main components of the problem?
2. Which parts are causes, which are effects, and which are background conditions?
3. Which parts are separable, and which are interdependent?
4. What is the best first cut: actors, processes, causes, time periods, decisions, incentives, or constraints?
5. Does the decomposition reveal the problem or distort it?
6. What disappears when the problem is broken into parts?
7. Which part of the problem carries the most uncertainty?
8. Which part carries the greatest practical consequence?
9. Which part is easiest to measure but least important?
10. Which part is hardest to measure but most important?
11. Are we decomposing the problem according to how the world works or how an institution is organized?
12. Are we confusing the administrative structure of a problem with its causal structure?
13. What relationships among parts matter most?
14. Which part is a bottleneck?
15. Which part is a leverage point?

## 2.2 Choosing a Representation

1. What kind of representation best fits the problem: map, table, matrix, timeline, tree, flowchart, causal diagram, system map, or narrative?
2. What does this representation make visible?
3. What does this representation hide?
4. Does the diagram clarify relationships or merely make the analysis look organized?
5. Are the links in the representation causal, temporal, logical, institutional, or associative?
6. Are the categories in the representation mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive?
7. What would change if the same issue were represented as a process rather than a list?
8. What would change if it were represented as a system rather than a linear chain?
9. What would change if it were represented as a decision tree rather than a narrative?
10. Does the representation show uncertainty, or does it imply more certainty than exists?
11. Does the representation show feedback, delay, or interaction?
12. Is the representation too simple for the problem or too complex to be useful?
13. Who might disagree with the representation, and why?
14. Could a different representation support a different recommendation?
15. How should the representation be revised as new evidence appears?

## 2.3 Boundaries and Variables

1. What is inside the analysis, and what is outside it?
2. What boundary choice is being made, and who benefits from it?
3. Which variables are inputs, outputs, constraints, actors, incentives, or background conditions?
4. Which variables are controllable, and which are merely observable?
5. Which variables are treated as fixed even though they may change?
6. Which variables are endogenous to the system rather than external to it?
7. Are we leaving out a slow-moving variable that matters over time?
8. Are we overemphasizing a visible variable because it is easy to measure?
9. Which variable would be most useful to know but is currently unavailable?
10. Which variables might be proxies for deeper forces?
11. Are there hidden dependencies among the variables?
12. Which variables might interact rather than operate independently?
13. Which variables are likely to be affected by the intervention itself?
14. Are we analyzing the right unit: person, group, organization, event, location, time period, or system?
15. What would an alternative boundary reveal that the current one hides?

# 3. Computational and Algorithmic Thinking

## 3.1 Problems as Procedures

1. Can this problem be described as a sequence of steps?
2. What happens first, next, and after that?
3. Where do decisions enter the process?
4. What conditions trigger different paths?
5. Where does the process loop, repeat, or require feedback?
6. Which step is most likely to fail?
7. Which step depends on information that may be incomplete or wrong?
8. Which step requires judgment rather than rule-following?
9. Which step could be automated, and which should not be?
10. Does proceduralizing the problem clarify responsibility?
11. Does proceduralizing the problem hide discretion, ambiguity, or ethical judgment?
12. What would happen if this procedure were followed rigidly?
13. What exceptions should the procedure anticipate?
14. What information must be available at each step for the procedure to work?
15. How would the procedure change under time pressure, resource scarcity, or uncertainty?

## 3.2 Conditions, Loops, and Constraints

1. What are the relevant if-then conditions?
2. Which conditions are explicit, and which are tacit?
3. Are there decision rules that need to be stated more clearly?
4. What condition would cause the analysis to stop, restart, escalate, or revise?
5. What feedback should be collected after each iteration?
6. What constraints limit the possible solution space?
7. Which constraints are real, and which are assumed?
8. Which constraints are ethical, legal, political, technical, financial, or cognitive?
9. Which constraint binds first?
10. What tradeoff is created by the binding constraint?
11. Are we optimizing within the wrong constraints?
12. Are we ignoring a constraint because it is inconvenient?
13. What would a good-enough solution look like under these constraints?
14. When is satisficing more rational than optimizing?
15. When does the search for the optimal answer become a source of delay or failure?

## 3.3 Algorithmic Thinking and Human Judgment

1. What can algorithmic thinking clarify in this problem?
2. What human judgment does it risk oversimplifying?
3. What assumptions would an algorithm need to encode?
4. Who chooses the decision rule, and according to what values?
5. What happens when the rule encounters an unusual case?
6. What would be the cost of false positives and false negatives?
7. Does the procedure optimize for the right objective?
8. Could the procedure be gamed?
9. Could optimizing one metric degrade the broader goal?
10. What kinds of errors would the procedure make systematically?
11. What review or appeal mechanism would be needed?
12. Is transparency possible, and for whom?
13. Would a simple heuristic outperform a complex procedure in this environment?
14. What feedback would improve the procedure over time?
15. How should responsibility be assigned when a procedure guides a decision?

# 4. Decision Analysis

## 4.1 Comparing Options Under Uncertainty

1. What decision actually has to be made?
2. What are the available options?
3. Are there options that have not been considered?
4. What happens if we do nothing?
5. What are the likely consequences of each option?
6. What are the best-case, worst-case, and most likely outcomes?
7. What uncertainties matter most to the decision?
8. What information would be worth gathering before deciding?
9. What information would be interesting but not decision-relevant?
10. Are we comparing options against the same baseline?
11. Are we confusing the best option with the least uncomfortable option?
12. Are we choosing between options or choosing a strategy for learning?
13. What option preserves flexibility?
14. What option creates irreversible commitment?
15. What would make delaying the decision wise or irresponsible?

## 4.2 Tradeoffs, Utility, and Thresholds

1. What values are in tension?
2. What must be sacrificed to gain the desired outcome?
3. Are we treating all goals as equally important when they are not?
4. What outcome would be unacceptable even if the expected value were favorable?
5. What level of risk is tolerable given the stakes?
6. What is the action threshold?
7. What evidence would move us across that threshold?
8. What would make one option clearly dominate another?
9. Where do reasonable people disagree: facts, probabilities, values, or risk tolerance?
10. Are we using expected value where precaution, rights, fairness, or resilience should matter more?
11. Are small probabilities of severe harm being dismissed too easily?
12. Are vivid harms being overweighted relative to likely benefits?
13. What distributional effects matter: who gains, who loses, and who bears risk?
14. What is the opportunity cost of choosing this option?
15. Does the recommendation acknowledge tradeoffs honestly?

## 4.3 Sensitivity and Robustness

1. Which assumption matters most to the recommendation?
2. If that assumption changes, does the recommendation change?
3. What range of plausible values should be considered?
4. Is the conclusion robust across plausible scenarios?
5. Is the conclusion fragile but being presented as firm?
6. Which uncertainty is most worth reducing?
7. Which uncertainty cannot be reduced in time for the decision?
8. What would the recommendation be under pessimistic assumptions?
9. What would the recommendation be under optimistic assumptions?
10. What would the recommendation be under a rival value weighting?
11. Are we mistaking precise calculations for robust reasoning?
12. What hidden assumption is doing the most work?
13. What evidence would make the current option no longer acceptable?
14. What monitoring plan would detect if assumptions are failing?
15. How should the recommendation be revised if early indicators go badly?

# 5. Process and Systems Orientation

## 5.1 Process Thinking

1. What process produces the outcome we are observing?
2. Where does the process begin and end?
3. What are the stages, handoffs, and decision points?
4. Where does information enter the process?
5. Where can information be lost, distorted, delayed, or ignored?
6. Which handoff is most fragile?
7. Which step creates the most delay or rework?
8. Are errors caused by individuals, process design, incentives, or coordination failures?
9. What failure would be expected given the structure of the process?
10. What would the process look like from the perspective of the user, client, citizen, or patient?
11. What informal workarounds keep the formal process functioning?
12. What does the process reward, punish, or ignore?
13. Which part of the process is optimized locally but harmful globally?
14. What data would reveal whether the process is improving?
15. What redesign would reduce friction without creating new failure points?

## 5.2 Systems Thinking

1. What are the interacting parts of the system?
2. What makes this a system rather than a collection of parts?
3. What feedback loops are present?
4. Which feedback loops stabilize the system?
5. Which feedback loops amplify change?
6. Where are there delays between cause and effect?
7. What unintended consequences might follow from the proposed intervention?
8. Which intervention treats a symptom rather than a structural driver?
9. Where is the leverage point?
10. Are we pushing harder on a low-leverage intervention?
11. What behavior does the system produce over time?
12. What does the system make easy, hard, visible, or invisible?
13. How might actors adapt once the intervention is introduced?
14. What boundary choice changes the system story most significantly?
15. What would happen if everyone responded rationally to local incentives?

## 5.3 Complexity, Simplification, and Humility

1. What simplification is necessary for analysis to proceed?
2. What risk does that simplification create?
3. Are we simplifying structure, time, actors, values, incentives, or uncertainty?
4. Is the model simple enough to use but rich enough to matter?
5. Which interactions are being ignored?
6. Which nonlinear effects might appear only after a threshold is crossed?
7. Which second-order effects are most plausible?
8. Which delayed consequences might be mistaken for unrelated events?
9. What would make this analysis obsolete?
10. Where should the analysis explicitly say, “we do not know”?
11. What failure mode would be created by overconfidence in the model?
12. What would an adaptive strategy look like instead of a fixed solution?
13. What signs would show that the system is responding differently than expected?
14. What kind of learning loop should be built into implementation?
15. How can analysis remain useful without pretending to fully control the system?

# 6. Meta-Analytical Habits

## 6.1 Analyzing the Analysis

1. What method am I using, and why is it appropriate here?
2. What does this method help me see?
3. What does this method make easy to overlook?
4. What assumptions does this method require?
5. What would a different method reveal?
6. Have I confused the structure of my analysis with the structure of reality?
7. Am I searching for evidence or confirming a preferred interpretation?
8. What would I expect to see if my conclusion were wrong?
9. Have I considered the strongest rival explanation?
10. What would a competent critic say is missing?
11. Where am I most tempted to overstate confidence?
12. Where am I relying on a convenient proxy?
13. What evidence am I discounting too quickly?
14. What part of the analysis is most vulnerable to bias?
15. How would I revise the analysis if I had to argue the opposite case?

## 6.2 Assumption Audits

1. What are the central assumptions?
2. Which assumptions are necessary, and which are merely helpful?
3. Which assumptions are testable?
4. Which assumptions are inherited from the problem frame?
5. Which assumptions come from disciplinary habits?
6. Which assumptions come from institutional incentives?
7. Which assumption is most uncertain?
8. Which assumption is most consequential?
9. Which assumption is most likely to be challenged by stakeholders?
10. Which assumption would be invisible to someone inside the system but obvious to an outsider?
11. What evidence supports each assumption?
12. What evidence would undermine each assumption?
13. Are any assumptions actually value judgments?
14. Are any assumptions treated as fixed because changing them would be politically difficult?
15. How should the conclusion be qualified given the assumptions?

## 6.3 Alternative Analysis and Intellectual Humility

1. What else could be true?
2. What is the best rival interpretation?
3. What evidence supports the rival interpretation?
4. What would the world look like if the rival interpretation were correct?
5. Have we considered both accidental and intentional explanations?
6. Have we considered structural explanations rather than only individual explanations?
7. Have we considered incentives rather than only stated intentions?
8. Have we considered error, noise, and chance rather than only meaningful patterns?
9. What explanation is being rejected too quickly because it is uncomfortable?
10. What explanation is being accepted too quickly because it is familiar?
11. What would make us change our minds?
12. Are we humble enough about uncertainty but clear enough to act?
13. Are we using uncertainty as an excuse for inaction?
14. Are we using confidence as an excuse to ignore revision?
15. What practice would make this analysis more self-correcting?

# 7. Program Evaluation and Policy Analysis

## 7.1 Evaluation Questions and Theories of Change

1. What exactly is the program or policy supposed to accomplish?
2. For whom is it supposed to work?
3. Under what conditions is it supposed to work?
4. Through what mechanism is it supposed to work?
5. What problem is the intervention actually trying to solve?
6. Is the intervention aligned with the stated problem?
7. What assumptions connect inputs to activities, activities to outputs, outputs to outcomes, and outcomes to impacts?
8. Which part of the theory of change is weakest?
9. What would count as evidence that the intervention is working?
10. What would count as evidence that it is failing?
11. What unintended consequences should be monitored?
12. Is the theory of change plausible to those affected by the intervention?
13. Are there multiple pathways to success or failure?
14. Does the theory of change confuse activity with impact?
15. What would an honest logic model reveal that a promotional description hides?

## 7.2 Outputs, Outcomes, and Impacts

1. What was produced?
2. What changed?
3. What mattered?
4. Are outputs being used as substitutes for outcomes?
5. Are outcomes being assumed rather than measured?
6. Are short-term indicators being mistaken for long-term impact?
7. Who experiences the outcome?
8. Who bears the cost?
9. Are average effects hiding unequal effects?
10. Are easy-to-measure outcomes crowding out important but difficult outcomes?
11. What baseline is being used?
12. What would have happened without the intervention?
13. Is there evidence of implementation failure rather than theory failure?
14. What would success look like from different stakeholder perspectives?
15. Does the evaluation distinguish effectiveness, equity, efficiency, feasibility, and legitimacy?

## 7.3 From Evidence to Recommendation

1. What options are available to decision-makers?
2. What criteria should be used to compare them?
3. Which criteria are empirical, and which are value-laden?
4. What recommendation follows from the evidence?
5. How strong is the evidence behind the recommendation?
6. What caveats should accompany the recommendation?
7. What implementation constraints matter?
8. What political or institutional realities might affect success?
9. Who must act for the recommendation to work?
10. What incentives must align?
11. What risks does the recommendation introduce?
12. What monitoring should follow implementation?
13. What would trigger revision or termination?
14. Does the recommendation overpromise?
15. Is the recommendation useful enough for action while honest enough about uncertainty?

# 8. Intelligence Analysis and Structured Analytic Techniques

## 8.1 Inference Under Ambiguity

1. What do we know?
2. What do we think we know?
3. What do we not know?
4. What might be deliberately hidden or deceptive?
5. What information is ambiguous rather than merely missing?
6. What is signal, and what is noise?
7. What would make this signal more diagnostic?
8. What evidence is absent but expected?
9. What missing information matters most?
10. Are we interpreting silence correctly?
11. Are we overfitting a story to sparse information?
12. Are we underestimating the possibility of deception?
13. What indicators would distinguish one hypothesis from another?
14. How should confidence be stated given incomplete information?
15. What would responsible warning look like under uncertainty?

## 8.2 Competing Hypotheses

1. What are the plausible hypotheses?
2. Have we included enough hypotheses, including uncomfortable ones?
3. Which hypothesis best explains the evidence?
4. Which evidence is most diagnostic across hypotheses?
5. Which evidence is consistent with all hypotheses and therefore less useful?
6. Which hypothesis survives the most serious challenges?
7. Which hypothesis are we favoring too early?
8. Which hypothesis are we dismissing because it is inconvenient?
9. What evidence would strongly disconfirm the leading hypothesis?
10. Are we explaining evidence after the fact rather than predicting it beforehand?
11. What assumptions must be true for each hypothesis?
12. Which hypothesis has the fewest unsupported assumptions?
13. Which hypothesis best accounts for anomalies?
14. What indicators should be monitored going forward?
15. How should the conclusion communicate confidence and alternatives?

## 8.3 Structured Challenge

1. What key assumption should be challenged first?
2. What would a devil’s advocate argue?
3. What would a red team attack?
4. What would someone with different incentives notice?
5. What would an adversary want us to believe?
6. What evidence might be planted, distorted, or selectively revealed?
7. Where are we vulnerable to mirror imaging?
8. Where are we relying on conventional wisdom?
9. What would happen if the opposite of our assumption were true?
10. What early warning indicators would signal failure of the current assessment?
11. What alternative future should be taken seriously even if unlikely?
12. What uncertainty should be escalated rather than buried?
13. Are caveats clear, or are they used to avoid making a judgment?
14. Is the confidence level calibrated to evidence quality?
15. Does the analytic product distinguish evidence, inference, judgment, and confidence?

# 9. Data Analysis, Visualization, and Evidentiary Displays

## 9.1 Questions to Ask of Data

1. What is being counted or measured?
2. Who or what is included in the dataset?
3. Who or what is excluded?
4. Over what time period were the data collected?
5. What categories were used, and who defined them?
6. What is the unit of analysis?
7. What is the comparison group?
8. What is the denominator?
9. Are we looking at counts, rates, percentages, averages, or distributions?
10. What is missing?
11. Is missingness random or meaningful?
12. What is the source of the data?
13. What transformations have been applied?
14. What uncertainty is shown, and what uncertainty is hidden?
15. What inference is the data being asked to support?

## 9.2 Descriptive Analysis

1. What pattern is visible before modeling?
2. What trend appears over time?
3. What differences appear across groups?
4. What outliers or anomalies deserve attention?
5. Are outliers errors, rare events, or important signals?
6. Are averages hiding variation?
7. Are group comparisons fair and meaningful?
8. Are rates more appropriate than raw counts?
9. Are absolute differences more informative than relative differences?
10. Are changes meaningful or merely visually dramatic?
11. What baseline or reference point is being used?
12. What alternative summary would tell a different story?
13. What would a distribution show that an average hides?
14. What would a time-series show that a snapshot hides?
15. What descriptive fact is most decision-relevant?

## 9.3 Visual Reasoning and Misleading Displays

1. What is the visual trying to make salient?
2. Does the chart type fit the question?
3. Does the scale exaggerate or minimize the pattern?
4. Is the baseline appropriate?
5. Are categories ordered meaningfully?
6. Are colors, icons, or design choices carrying rhetorical weight?
7. Is the visual cluttering the analysis or clarifying it?
8. Does the visual show uncertainty?
9. Does it show context?
10. Does it invite a causal interpretation that is not justified?
11. Does it hide denominators or sample size?
12. Does it compare unlike things?
13. What would the same data look like in another chart form?
14. What would a skeptical redesign include?
15. What conclusion would a viewer likely draw, and is that conclusion warranted?

# 10. Risk Analysis and Uncertainty Communication

## 10.1 Characterizing Risk

1. What is the hazard?
2. Who or what is exposed?
3. What is the likelihood?
4. What is the consequence?
5. What is the vulnerability?
6. What is known, uncertain, ambiguous, or unknown?
7. Are we dealing with measurable risk or deeper uncertainty?
8. What is the time horizon?
9. What is the worst credible outcome?
10. What is the most likely outcome?
11. What outcome would be unacceptable even if unlikely?
12. What risks are being transferred from one group to another?
13. What risks are visible now but delayed later?
14. What risk is being ignored because it is hard to quantify?
15. What risk is being exaggerated because it is vivid?

## 10.2 Low-Probability, High-Consequence Events

1. How should rare but severe possibilities be handled?
2. What evidence is available about low-frequency events?
3. Are we underweighting a catastrophic possibility because it has not happened recently?
4. Are we overweighting a dramatic possibility because it is memorable?
5. What would resilience require?
6. What would precaution require?
7. What is the cost of false alarm?
8. What is the cost of missed warning?
9. What monitoring would improve early detection?
10. What contingency plans are justified?
11. What assumptions about normality may fail under stress?
12. What second-order effects could turn a small disruption into a major failure?
13. How should decision thresholds change when consequences are irreversible?
14. What would robust action look like across multiple possible futures?
15. How should uncertainty be communicated without producing panic or complacency?

## 10.3 Risk Communication

1. Who is the audience?
2. What decision do they need to make?
3. What do they need to know, and what might distract them?
4. Should risk be communicated as probabilities, frequencies, scenarios, comparisons, or narratives?
5. Are absolute risks and relative risks clearly distinguished?
6. Are uncertainty and confidence clearly distinguished?
7. Is the communication transparent about what is unknown?
8. Does the communication hide value judgments behind technical language?
9. Is the communication designed to inform or to persuade?
10. What misunderstanding is most likely?
11. What context would prevent exaggeration?
12. What context would prevent minimization?
13. How should disagreement among experts be represented?
14. How should recommendations be separated from evidence?
15. What would honest, actionable, non-alarmist communication look like?

# 11. Structured Problem Solving and Root Cause Analysis

## 11.1 Defining the Problem

1. What exactly is wrong?
2. Relative to what expectation or standard is it wrong?
3. Who experiences it as a problem?
4. Who benefits from the current state?
5. Is the problem chronic, acute, recurring, or emerging?
6. What changed before the problem appeared?
7. What has stayed the same?
8. What evidence shows that the problem exists?
9. What would show that the problem has improved?
10. Are we analyzing the right level: individual, team, process, organization, market, system, or culture?
11. Are we mistaking a visible symptom for the underlying problem?
12. Are we defining the problem in terms of a preferred solution?
13. What would the problem look like to a frontline participant?
14. What would it look like to a leader or decision-maker?
15. What would it look like to someone affected but not consulted?

## 11.2 Causes, Symptoms, and Causal Chains

1. What is the symptom?
2. What is the proximate cause?
3. What is the deeper cause?
4. What is the systemic cause?
5. What causal chain links them?
6. Where does the chain have weak evidence?
7. Where might there be multiple causes?
8. Where might causes interact?
9. What alternative causal story fits the same facts?
10. Are we stopping the analysis at the first plausible cause?
11. Are we blaming individuals for a process problem?
12. Are we blaming process for a values or incentives problem?
13. Are we ignoring constraints that make failure predictable?
14. What evidence would distinguish root cause from coincidence?
15. Which cause is actionable without being superficial?

## 11.3 Root Cause Tools and Recommendations

1. What does a 5 Whys analysis reveal?
2. Where might a 5 Whys analysis oversimplify?
3. What does a fishbone diagram reveal that a linear chain hides?
4. What does a fault tree reveal about combinations of failures?
5. Which tool fits the problem best?
6. Which causes are controllable?
7. Which causes are high-impact?
8. Which causes are low-cost to address?
9. Which intervention addresses root causes rather than symptoms?
10. What intervention might create new problems?
11. What criteria should rank possible interventions?
12. What tradeoffs exist among speed, cost, fairness, durability, and risk?
13. What implementation obstacles should be expected?
14. What metrics would show whether the fix worked?
15. What review cycle should follow the intervention?

## 12.1 Forecasting as Disciplined Judgment

2. Over what time horizon?
3. What would count as the event occurring?
4. What is the base rate?
5. What reference class is most appropriate?
6. What trend information matters?
7. What current indicators matter?

12. What uncertainty is reducible, and what is not?
