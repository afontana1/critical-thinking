# Critical Questions for Systems Thinking

## Course-Level Critical Questions

### Seeing Systems

1. What makes this situation a system rather than merely a collection of separate parts?
2. What pattern of behavior over time needs to be explained?
3. Which elements, relationships, and flows are most important to the system's behavior?
4. What changes when we shift from asking “Who caused this?” to “What structure produced this?”
5. What would a purely reductionist analysis notice, and what would it miss?
6. What would a systems analysis notice that a variable-by-variable analysis might overlook?
7. What is the system currently producing, regardless of what it officially claims to be designed to produce?
8. What recurring behavior suggests that the problem is structural rather than accidental?
9. Where are local improvements producing global harm?
10. What unintended consequences suggest that the original problem frame was too narrow?

### Systems Thinking as Critical Thinking

1. What assumptions are built into the way this system has been described?
2. Which boundary choices make the problem look simple, and which make it look more complex?
3. What actors, incentives, or feedback loops disappear when the system boundary is drawn too tightly?
4. What would count as evidence that our system map is misleading?
5. Are we using a systems concept to clarify the situation, or merely to make the analysis sound sophisticated?
6. Where might a beautiful diagram create false confidence?
7. What are we treating as fixed that may actually be variable?
8. What are we treating as external that may actually be part of the system?
9. What are we treating as a cause that may actually be an effect of a deeper structure?
10. What kind of humility does systems thinking require?

## Unit 1: From Parts to Wholes

### Reductionism, Holism, and Interaction

1. What parts of the problem can be understood by isolating components, and what parts require seeing interactions?
2. When does breaking a problem into parts make understanding easier, and when does it distort the phenomenon?
3. What properties of the whole are not visible in any single part?
4. How do the parts change one another through interaction?
5. Are we confusing a component failure with a system failure?
6. What would change if we analyzed the relationships rather than the elements?
7. How does the same component behave differently in different systems?
8. What evidence suggests that the system is producing outcomes no individual actor intended?
9. Where does linear cause-and-effect thinking break down?
10. What kind of problem requires analytical decomposition first, and what kind requires systems framing first?

### Purpose and Behavior

1. What does the system say its purpose is?
2. What does the system's actual behavior suggest its purpose is?
3. Who benefits from the current pattern of behavior?
4. Who bears the costs of the current pattern of behavior?
5. What outputs are rewarded, measured, or protected?
6. What would have to change for the system's stated purpose and actual behavior to align?
7. Are dysfunctions really failures, or are they predictable outputs of the current structure?
8. What persistent pattern would remain even if a few individuals changed their behavior?
9. What does the system make easy, and what does it make difficult?
10. What does the system repeatedly reproduce despite attempts to change it?

## Unit 2: Systems Thinking as a Habit of Mind

### Stocks and Flows

1. What is accumulating or depleting in this system?
2. What are the inflows and outflows that change the stock over time?
3. Are we focusing too much on events and not enough on accumulation?
4. What stock is invisible but important, such as trust, skill, attention, debt, risk, or resentment?
5. How long would it take for a change in flow to noticeably affect the stock?
6. Are people reacting to the current stock, the current flow, or a delayed perception of one of them?
7. What happens if the inflow increases while the outflow is constrained?
8. What happens if the outflow increases faster than the stock can be replenished?
9. Which stock is being treated as infinite when it is actually limited?
10. Which stock is being depleted without being measured?

### Feedback Loops

1. What reinforcing loops amplify growth, decline, confidence, panic, or inequality?
2. What balancing loops stabilize the system or resist change?
3. Which feedback loop dominates in the short term?
4. Which feedback loop dominates in the long term?
5. What feedback is missing, delayed, distorted, or ignored?
6. How does the system learn from its own behavior?
7. How does the system prevent learning from its own behavior?
8. Are actors responding to real feedback or to a proxy measure?
9. Where does attempted correction create overcorrection?
10. What loop explains why small changes can grow into large consequences?

### Delays, Nonlinearity, and Boundaries

1. What important effects occur only after a delay?
2. How might delay cause actors to underestimate the consequences of their actions?
3. How might delay cause actors to overreact once consequences finally appear?
4. Where are there thresholds, tipping points, or saturation effects?
5. Which relationships are nonlinear rather than proportional?
6. What boundary have we drawn around the system?
7. Who or what is outside the boundary but still affected by the system?
8. What would become visible if we expanded the boundary?
9. What would become manageable if we narrowed the boundary?
10. Which level of analysis—individual, organizational, institutional, ecological—best explains the pattern?

### Leverage Points

1. Where are people trying to intervene now?
2. Is the intervention aimed at symptoms, flows, feedback, rules, goals, or paradigms?
3. Which change would alter the system's behavior with the least ongoing force?
4. Which intervention looks powerful but only changes a parameter?
5. Which information flow, if changed, would alter behavior across the system?
6. Which rule or incentive is quietly maintaining the unwanted pattern?
7. Which goal is the system optimizing for, explicitly or implicitly?
8. What would happen if the system's goal changed?
9. Where is a small intervention likely to be absorbed without much effect?
10. Where might a small intervention cascade through the system?

## Unit 3: Systems Science and General Systems Theory

### General Systems Principles

1. What general system principle helps explain this case: homeostasis, emergence, equifinality, self-organization, adaptation, or openness?
2. Are multiple pathways producing the same outcome?
3. Is the system maintaining stability even when actors are trying to change it?
4. What emergent pattern arises from local interactions?
5. Can the observed behavior be explained without assuming central control?
6. What is self-organizing here?
7. How do constraints shape the system's possible behaviors?
8. How does the system preserve its identity while changing over time?
9. What would we misunderstand if we treated this as a closed system?
10. What flows of matter, energy, information, money, attention, or trust cross the system boundary?

### Information and Control

1. What information regulates this system?
2. Who receives the information needed to act?
3. Who lacks information but bears consequences?
4. What information arrives too late to be useful?
5. What information is distorted by incentives, fear, noise, or measurement choices?
6. What control mechanisms stabilize the system?
7. What control mechanisms create rigidity or fragility?
8. How does the system detect error?
9. How does the system suppress error signals?
10. What would better information make possible?

## Unit 4: Systems Analysis

### Framing a Situation as a System

1. What behavior over time is the central puzzle?
2. What events are symptoms of a deeper pattern?
3. What stocks, flows, feedback loops, delays, and incentives might produce the pattern?
4. Who are the stakeholders, and how do their goals interact?
5. What structural features make the unwanted behavior likely?
6. What would a behavior-over-time graph reveal that a snapshot hides?
7. What historical pattern should be examined before proposing a solution?
8. What rival system story could explain the same behavior?
9. What data would help distinguish between rival system stories?
10. What kind of map or representation would best clarify the issue?

### Causal Loop Diagrams and Behavior-Over-Time Graphs

1. What variables belong in the causal loop diagram, and which would add clutter without insight?
2. Are the arrows causal, correlational, definitional, or merely speculative?
3. Which loops are reinforcing, and which are balancing?
4. What evidence supports each link in the diagram?
5. Where are delays located in the causal structure?
6. What behavior-over-time pattern would this diagram predict?
7. Does the actual pattern match the proposed structure?
8. What alternative diagram could generate the same pattern?
9. What does the diagram make visible?
10. What does the diagram hide?

### Structural Explanation Instead of Blame

1. What would a blame-based explanation say?
2. What would a structural explanation say?
3. How might good people produce bad outcomes under this structure?
4. What incentives make the harmful behavior reasonable from an actor's local perspective?
5. How does the system reward short-term rationality that harms long-term outcomes?
6. What recurring conditions keep producing the same failure?
7. What intervention would change the conditions rather than merely punish the behavior?
8. When is personal responsibility still relevant within a structural explanation?
9. How can structural analysis avoid excusing harmful action?
10. How can moral analysis and systems analysis strengthen rather than replace each other?

## Unit 5: System Design and Redesign

### Moving from Diagnosis to Intervention

1. What behavior do we want the system to produce instead?
2. What structure would make that behavior more likely?
3. What intervention targets the cause of the pattern rather than the symptom?
4. What change can be tested incrementally before scaling?
5. What early feedback would indicate that the intervention is working?
6. What early feedback would indicate that the intervention is creating new problems?
7. What unintended consequences should be anticipated?
8. What second-order effects might follow from the intervention?
9. What would make the redesigned system resilient rather than merely efficient?
10. How would the intervention affect different stakeholders differently?

### Incentives, Rules, Norms, and Information Flows

1. What incentives currently drive behavior?
2. Are incentives aligned with the stated goals of the system?
3. Which metric might be distorting behavior?
4. Which rule produces unintended consequences?
5. Which informal norm matters more than the formal policy?
6. What information is needed for better coordination?
7. Who needs to see what information, and when?
8. What feedback should be made more timely or visible?
9. What hidden cost or externality should be made visible?
10. Which design change would reduce the need for constant supervision?

### Ethics of System Design

1. Who has the authority to redesign the system?
2. Who is consulted before redesign occurs?
3. Who gains power from the proposed redesign?
4. Who loses autonomy, privacy, resources, or voice?
5. What burdens are shifted from one group to another?
6. What values are embedded in the proposed design?
7. What kind of transparency does the intervention require?
8. How can the redesign be made accountable to those affected by it?
9. Does the design respect human agency, or does it manipulate behavior invisibly?
10. What would a fair system design process require?

## Unit 6: System Archetypes

### Diagnosing Recurring Patterns

1. What system archetype does this case resemble?
2. What behavior-over-time pattern supports that diagnosis?
3. What feedback structure supports that diagnosis?
4. What evidence would show that the archetype is a poor fit?
5. Are we using the archetype as a hypothesis or forcing the case into a template?
6. What is the recurring short-term fix?
7. What long-term consequence does the fix produce?
8. What limit is constraining growth?
9. What burden is being shifted from a fundamental solution to a symptomatic solution?
10. What resource is being overused because individual incentives conflict with collective well-being?

### Archetype-Specific Questions

#### Fixes That Fail

1. What short-term solution appears to help?
2. What delayed consequence makes the problem worse?
3. Why does the fix remain attractive despite failure?
4. What fundamental solution is being postponed?
5. What feedback would reveal the fix's long-term cost?

#### Shifting the Burden

1. What symptomatic solution is being used repeatedly?
2. What deeper capacity or structural solution is being neglected?
3. How does dependence on the quick fix grow over time?
4. Who benefits from the symptomatic solution?
5. What would reduce reliance on the short-term fix?

#### Limits to Growth

1. What reinforcing process is producing growth?
2. What constraint eventually slows or reverses growth?
3. Is the constraint physical, social, cognitive, institutional, or ecological?
4. Are actors pushing harder on growth instead of addressing the constraint?
5. What leverage point would relax or transform the limiting condition?

#### Drifting Goals

1. What standard is gradually being lowered?
2. What pressure makes lowering the standard attractive?
3. How is the decline normalized over time?
4. What evidence would reveal the drift?
5. What mechanism could preserve the original goal?

#### Tragedy of the Commons

1. What shared resource is being depleted?
2. What individual incentive drives overuse?
3. Why does each actor's behavior seem reasonable locally?
4. What collective rule or norm could protect the commons?
5. What monitoring or feedback is needed for sustainability?

#### Success to the Successful

1. What early advantage is being amplified?
2. How does success attract more resources, attention, or legitimacy?
3. Who is excluded as the gap widens?
4. What feedback loop makes inequality self-reinforcing?
5. What intervention would prevent initial differences from compounding unfairly?

## Unit 7: System Dynamics

### Dynamic Patterns

1. What dynamic pattern is visible: growth, decay, goal-seeking, oscillation, overshoot, or collapse?
2. What feedback structure could generate that pattern?
3. What delay could make the system unstable?
4. What stock is being depleted during apparent success?
5. What balancing loop eventually limits growth?
6. What reinforcing loop accelerates decline?
7. What early warning signs precede overshoot?
8. What makes actors continue harmful behavior after the system has exceeded a limit?
9. What would a simulation help us test that intuition alone cannot?
10. What assumptions would need to be explicit before building a formal model?

### From Mental Models to Formal Models

1. What mental model are actors using to understand the system?
2. What assumptions does that mental model contain?
3. What behavior does the mental model predict?
4. Where does observed behavior contradict the mental model?
5. What would formal modeling clarify?
6. What would formal modeling risk oversimplifying?
7. Which variables are measurable, and which are important but hard to quantify?
8. How would different stakeholders model the same system differently?
9. What parameter changes would be worth testing?
10. How should model results be communicated without false precision?

## Unit 8: Complex Systems

### Complicated vs. Complex

1. Is this system complicated, complex, or both?
2. Are the components merely numerous, or are they adapting to one another?
3. Can the system be understood by complete analysis of its parts, or does interaction change the behavior?
4. Does intervention change the system's future behavior by changing the actors' strategies?
5. Is prediction difficult because of missing information or because the system is adaptive?
6. What kind of expertise is appropriate: engineering control, adaptive management, scenario thinking, or experimentation?
7. What failure would result from treating a complex system as merely complicated?
8. What failure would result from treating a complicated system as mysterious complexity?
9. What role do local interactions play in producing global patterns?
10. What forms of robustness matter more than optimization?

### Emergence, Networks, and Cascades

1. What large-scale pattern emerges from local behavior?
2. How do agents adapt to one another?
3. How does the environment change as agents act within it?
4. What network structure matters: hubs, bridges, clusters, weak ties, or bottlenecks?
5. What could trigger a cascade?
6. What makes the system resilient to shocks?
7. What makes the system fragile to shocks?
8. Where could contagion spread: disease, information, panic, behavior, failure, or trust?
9. What local intervention could have network-wide consequences?
10. What uncertainty should make us prefer reversible or robust interventions?

## Unit 9: Systems Engineering

### Mission, Requirements, and Interfaces

1. What mission is the system meant to accomplish?
2. What requirements follow from that mission?
3. Are the requirements clear, testable, and traceable?
4. Where do subsystem goals conflict with system-level goals?
5. What interfaces are most likely to fail?
6. How do technical, human, institutional, and environmental elements interact?
7. What assumptions are documented, and which remain implicit?
8. What verification and validation processes are needed?
9. What risks emerge across the system lifecycle?
10. How should later operational feedback reshape future design?

### Socio-Technical Systems

1. What human practices are necessary for the technical system to work?
2. What training, incentives, norms, or organizational routines does the design assume?
3. What failure would occur if the technical design worked but the social design failed?
4. What stakeholders define success differently?
5. How does usability affect system performance?
6. What maintenance or lifecycle issue is being underestimated?
7. What trade-offs exist between cost, performance, reliability, safety, equity, and schedule?
8. What hidden dependency could undermine the whole system?
9. What kind of documentation supports accountability?
10. How does systems engineering function as structured critical thinking?

## Unit 10: Integrating the Systems Lens

### Practical Systems Checklist

1. What behavior over time am I trying to explain?
2. What are the key stocks and flows?
3. What feedback loops matter most?
4. Where are the delays?
5. What boundaries am I drawing?
6. What levels of system are involved?
7. What archetype might help diagnose the pattern?
8. What leverage points are available?
9. What intervention might create unintended consequences?
10. What would make the system more adaptive, fair, and resilient?

### Pitfalls in Applying Systems Thinking

1. Am I mistaking the map for the system?
2. Am I using complexity as an excuse for inaction?
3. Am I making the system so complex that no decision is possible?
4. Am I imposing an archetype instead of testing it?
5. Am I ignoring power, conflict, and values in the name of structure?
6. Am I treating all stakeholders as equally able to shape the system?
7. Am I overconfident because the diagram looks elegant?
8. Am I neglecting evidence that contradicts the system story?
9. Am I assuming that every problem requires a systems solution?
10. Am I balancing humility about prediction with responsibility for action?

### Bridge to Model Thinking

1. What system story should a model represent?
2. What variables, boundaries, and relationships would the model include?
3. What simplifications would be acceptable for the model's purpose?
4. What assumptions would need to be stated explicitly?
5. What would the model help us see that informal reasoning misses?
6. What would the model hide or distort?
7. How would we test whether the model is useful?
8. What decisions should not be made from the model alone?
9. How should uncertainty be represented in the model?
10. What makes a model a tool rather than a truth?

## Cross-Course Integration Questions

### Connections to Analytical Thinking

1. When should a problem be decomposed, and when should it be reassembled into a system?
2. How do analytical maps differ from systems maps?
3. What is the relationship between root-cause analysis and systemic explanation?
4. When does identifying a root cause oversimplify a systemic pattern?
5. How do decision thresholds change when feedback and unintended consequences are considered?

### Connections to Causality

1. How do causal diagrams differ from causal loop diagrams?
2. When is a causal claim about a linear effect insufficient for understanding system behavior?
3. How do feedback loops complicate ordinary causal reasoning?
4. How can a system produce causal effects that reverse over time?
5. What kind of causal evidence is needed before redesigning a system?

### Connections to Data Literacy and Evidence

1. What data would reveal the system's behavior over time?
2. What measurements might distort the system by becoming targets?
3. What missing data would prevent accurate systems diagnosis?
4. What indicators would show whether an intervention is working?
5. How can data dashboards improve feedback, and how can they mislead?

### Connections to Heuristics and Biases

1. Which cognitive shortcuts make systems hard to understand?
2. How do availability, salience, and hindsight bias encourage event-based explanations?
3. How does linear thinking obscure feedback and delay?
4. How might groupthink reinforce a flawed system story?
5. What decision hygiene practices help teams reason better about systems?

### Connections to Argumentation

1. What argument is implied by a system map?
2. What evidence supports each causal link in the system story?
3. What objections would challenge the proposed system explanation?
4. What rival system interpretation should be considered?
5. How can systems diagrams be used rhetorically or manipulatively?

## Capstone-Oriented Critical Questions

### Systems Case Analysis

1. What issue will be analyzed as a system?
2. What behavior-over-time pattern is central to the case?
3. What are the key elements, stocks, flows, and feedback loops?
4. What boundary choices are possible, and why choose one rather than another?
5. What stakeholders are inside and outside the map?
6. What archetype, if any, helps explain the pattern?
7. What evidence supports the diagnosis?
8. What evidence complicates or challenges the diagnosis?
9. What leverage points are plausible?
10. What redesign would change structure rather than merely address symptoms?

### Intervention and Redesign

1. What intervention is proposed?
2. What system structure does it target?
3. What short-term effects are expected?
4. What long-term effects are possible?
5. What unintended consequences are most plausible?
6. What feedback would help monitor the intervention?
7. What would make the intervention reversible or adjustable?
8. Who would be empowered or burdened by the intervention?
9. What ethical constraints should guide redesign?
10. What would count as success over time?

### Reflective Systems Practice

1. How did my initial framing change during the analysis?
2. What did I first treat as a cause that later appeared to be a symptom?
3. What boundary choice most shaped my conclusions?
4. What feedback loop was hardest to recognize?
5. What stakeholder perspective changed the system story?
6. What part of my diagram is most speculative?
7. What evidence would most improve the analysis?
8. What did I leave out for simplicity?
9. What are the risks of acting on my analysis?
10. What systems-thinking habit will I carry into future courses?

## Short Reusable Question Sets

### The Five-Minute Systems Scan

1. What pattern is repeating?
2. What stock is accumulating or depleting?
3. What feedback loop reinforces the pattern?
4. What delay hides the consequences?
5. What boundary choice changes the diagnosis?
6. What incentive maintains the behavior?
7. What information is missing or distorted?
8. What quick fix might make things worse?
9. What leverage point is being ignored?
10. What unintended consequence should we anticipate?

### The Systems Redesign Scan

1. What behavior do we want instead?
2. What structure would produce that behavior?
3. What feedback needs to change?
4. What rule, norm, incentive, or information flow matters most?
5. What intervention is small enough to test?
6. What would we monitor over time?
7. What group could be harmed by the redesign?
8. What could adapt in response to the intervention?
9. What would make the change resilient?
10. What would make us revise or abandon the intervention?

### The Anti-Overconfidence Scan

1. What if our map is wrong?
2. What if the boundary is wrong?
3. What if the archetype is wrong?
4. What if the leverage point is weaker than we think?
5. What if the intervention changes incentives in an unexpected way?
6. What if the system adapts against the intervention?
7. What if short-term success creates long-term fragility?
8. What if excluded stakeholders see something we missed?
9. What if our preferred solution is another “fix that fails”?
10. What evidence would force us to redraw the system?
